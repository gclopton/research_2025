

## Introduction

Swift-heavy-ion (SHI) irradiation produces highly localized, nanometer-scale damage tracks in many ionic and ceramic solids through predominantly electronic energy loss. In nuclear fuels and structural ceramics, such tracks and their associated defect populations influence thermal transport, swelling and stress evolution, and long-term microstructural stability under irradiation. Actinide oxides such as $UO(_2)$ are central materials in this context, but systematic, mechanism-resolving studies are complicated by radiological constraints. Cerium oxide is therefore widely used as a surrogate because it shares the fluorite crystal structure with $UO(_2)$ and exhibits comparable elastic and thermal responses while retaining a chemically active oxygen sublattice and a multivalent cation sublattice capable of reduction and reoxidation. These features make the Ce–O system an enabling platform for isolating and interrogating irradiation mechanisms that are otherwise difficult to probe directly in real fuels.

A persistent experimental theme in ceria is that SHI tracks often remain largely crystalline and only weakly underdense, even when track contrast is clearly resolved. Multiple studies report nanometer-scale track cores, modest density deficits, and signatures consistent with oxygen disorder and partial reduction of Ce($^{4+}$) to Ce($^{3+}$) in the track region. These observations motivate a physically specific hypothesis: ceria’s redox flexibility and oxygen nonstoichiometry are not merely byproducts of the spike, but active pathways that can modify defect energetics, oxygen redistribution, and recovery kinetics, thereby altering the final latent-track morphology and its evolution. The central open question is not whether reduction can occur, but under what irradiation and microstructural conditions redox/nonstoichiometry measurably reshapes track observables (core/shell structure, oxygen redistribution length scales, swelling/microstrain signatures, and early recovery).

Existing theoretical and computational treatments provide important but incomplete pieces of this picture. Continuum thermal-spike frameworks and two-temperature models (TTM) capture the division of deposited energy between an excited electronic subsystem and the lattice, and they rationalize track thresholds and velocity effects through electron diffusion and electron–phonon coupling. Atomistic simulations, including TTM-coupled molecular dynamics (TTM-MD/2T-MD), resolve defect creation, clustering, and melt–quench pathways. However, the ability to attribute ceria-specific behavior to redox requires a model stack that simultaneously (i) constrains the electron-to-lattice heating history from physically justified stopping and radial dose inputs, (ii) remains stable and accurate across crystalline, defect-rich, and high-temperature disordered environments, and (iii) represents redox/nonstoichiometry pathways with sufficient fidelity to permit controlled “redox-enabled versus redox-frozen” comparisons. Prior two-temperature MD studies using rigid-ion Buckingham–Coulomb force fields provide a useful baseline for spike-driven defect production and finite-size sensitivity, but they also illustrate key limitations: fixed-charge models do not represent explicit $Ce(^{3+})/Ce(^{4+})$ charge transfer, track-radius inference can be contaminated by boundary and thermostat placement, and the parameter space associated with stopping power, source width, and coupling must be treated systematically to separate physical trends from artifacts.

The present study develops an end-to-end, multiscale computational framework designed to quantify how redox and nonstoichiometry modify SHI track formation and early evolution in ceria. Stopping powers ($S_e(z)$) and ($S_n(z)$) are obtained for experimentally relevant ion energies using SRIM, and a velocity-class–dependent radial dose model ($S(r,t)$) is constructed with an explicit source width ($r_0$) and tail behavior. A standalone cylindrical TTM/i-TS solver is used to calibrate and verify the electron–lattice transport parameterization (${C_e(T_e), \kappa_e(T_e), g(T_e,T_l)}$) and to ensure that the coupled on-the-fly LAMMPS electron field reproduces consistent ($T_e(r,t)$) and ($T_l(r,t)$) envelopes under matched inputs. Production track simulations are then carried out with TTM-coupled MD in LAMMPS (electron grid diffusion + electron–phonon coupling), using simulation domains and boundary conditions chosen to separate the latent core from the heat-affected zone and to avoid spurious quenching by nearby thermostatted regions.

A central objective is to resolve how conclusions depend on the interaction model, particularly its ability to represent reduced chemistry. Accordingly, the computational campaign is constructed around controlled inter-model comparisons under identical TTM forcing. Classical baselines include (i) high-temperature thermomechanics-oriented many-body actinide-oxide potentials, (ii) benchmark rigid-ion Buckingham–Coulomb models with short-range stabilization appropriate for extreme environments, and (iii) reactive/charge-equilibrated force fields parameterized for $CeO_{2}$ and reduced ceria. In parallel, machine-learned interatomic potentials (MLIPs) are developed from DFT(+U) reference data generated with VASP. The MLIP training corpus spans the stoichiometry ladder $CeO(_2), Ce(_{11})O(_{20}), Ce(_7)O(_{12})$, and $Ce(_2)O(_3)$ (selected polymorph family), together with defect families (vacancies, interstitials, Frenkel pairs, clusters), strained states for thermoelastic response, and high-temperature disordered snapshots representing melt and quench environments. Two complementary MLIP classes are targeted: a strictly local, MPI-scalable Allegro model for large-scale TTM-MD track simulations, and a charge-equilibrated high-dimensional neural-network potential (4G-HDNNP with iterative QEq via n2p2) to represent global charge redistribution and electrostatics. Redox signatures are quantified using charge-based metrics in charge-equilibrated models and DFT-calibrated oxidation-state inference where required for local MLIPs.

By combining physically constrained energy deposition and electron–lattice transport with interaction models spanning rigid-ion, reactive, and ML descriptions, the study aims to establish how redox-driven defect chemistry and oxygen nonstoichiometry couple back into track morphology and evolution in ceria across stoichiometry, microstructure, and irradiation-condition space.


Swift heavy ions (SHIs) deposit energy into condensed matter predominantly through electronic stopping, producing transient thermal spikes that can generate nanometer-scale latent tracks in ionic ceramics. In fluorite oxides, and especially in ceria, track formation and early recovery appear to be strongly coupled to oxygen nonstoichiometry and cation reduction. A consistent experimental picture is that ceria often retains crystallinity while exhibiting modest density deficits, oxygen disorder, and signatures of partial Ce ${ }^{4+} \rightarrow \mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ reduction in the track region. These observations motivate a mechanism-level hypothesis in which redox flexibility and oxygen nonstoichiometry are active pathways that reshape defect energetics, oxygen redistribution, and recovery kinetics, thereby modifying latent-track morphology.

The proposed work develops an end-to-end, multiscale computational framework that constrains the electron-to-lattice heating history and isolates how redox/nonstoichiometry alters track morphology. Stopping powers $S_e(z)$ and $S_n(z)$ are obtained for experimentally relevant ion conditions using SRIM, and a velocity-class-dependent radial dose model $S(r, t)$ is specified via an explicit source width $r_0$ and tail behavior. Electron-lattice transport is represented through a cylindrical two-temperature model (TTM/i-TS), used as a calibration and verification layer for the coupled TTM implementation in LAMMPS. Production simulations are performed as TTM-coupled molecular dynamics (2T-MD/TTM-MD) in large, bulk-like periodic cells with boundary and sink regions placed sufficiently far from the track axis to avoid artificial quenching of the heat-affected zone. The primary composition ladder includes $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ and reduced fluorite-related ordered-vacancy phases $\mathrm{Ce}_{11} \mathrm{O}_{20}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_7 \mathrm{O}_{12}$, together with $\mathrm{Ce}_2 \mathrm{O}_3$ as a strongly reduced endpoint used for validation and as a potential long-time/overlap outcome.

A controlled comparison is carried out by running the same TTM-driven track protocol with multiple interaction models. The classical force fields include a rigid-ion Buckingham-Coulomb description (augmented with short-range stabilization) and a reactive, charge-equilibrated ReaxFF parameterization for $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ and reduced ceria. In parallel, machine-learned interatomic potentials are trained on VASP DFT(+U) reference data drawn from strained crystals, representative defect configurations (vacancies, interstitials, Frenkel pairs, and clusters), and high-temperature disordered structures spanning melt and quench conditions. Two complementary ML approaches are pursued. One is an MPI-scalable Allegro potential for large-scale TTM-MD simulations, and the other is a charge-equilibrated neural-network potential, 4G-HDNNP with iterative QEq via n2p2, which incorporates long-range charge redistribution and electrostatics. These simulations are used to track how core/shell radii, radial oxygen nonstoichiometry $\delta(r)$, swelling and microstrain signatures, defect clustering, and early recovery vary with irradiation condition and chemical state, providing a direct route to identifying when redox-driven defect chemistry alters track formation and evolution.
