
# Introduction

Swift heavy ions (SHIs) provide a distinct irradiation regime in which the dominant energy-loss channel in an insulating ceramic is electronic stopping rather than direct ballistic knock-on collisions. As the ion traverses the solid at high velocity, it excites and ionizes electrons along a narrow cylindrical region around its path, producing a dense electronic excitation that relaxes on ultrafast time scales through electron–electron scattering, electron transport, and energy transfer to the lattice. The practical consequence is a highly localized, transient thermal spike: a region near the ion path is driven to extreme effective temperatures for a few picoseconds and then rapidly quenched by heat diffusion into the surrounding crystal. Even when the material is not driven fully amorphous, this sequence can generate point defects, defect clusters, and nanometer-scale latent tracks—structural modifications whose radius, density deficit, and crystalline retention control macroscopic responses such as swelling, stress evolution, thermal transport degradation, and microstructural stability.

Fluorite oxides are central to this problem because their defect chemistry and recovery pathways are strongly coupled to the anion sublattice. Cerium oxide, in particular, is widely used as a chemically accessible surrogate for actinide dioxides: it shares the fluorite structure and comparable thermomechanical response, yet retains a reactive oxygen sublattice and a multivalent cation sublattice capable of $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ reduction and reoxidation. Oxygen vacancies in ceria are not simply missing atoms; they are accompanied by two excess electrons that, in a realistic description, localize on nearby Ce sites to form $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-like centers with occupied $4f$ character. This vacancy–polaron coupling means that any irradiation event that redistributes oxygen or changes local density can also change the local redox state, potentially altering vacancy formation energies, migration barriers, and the thermodynamic driving forces that govern defect annealing during quench.

Experimentally, SHI tracks in ceria are often resolvable yet remain largely crystalline and only modestly underdense, with reports of oxygen disorder/redistribution and partial $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}\rightarrow \mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ reduction in the track region. This combination—clear nanoscale track signatures without wholesale amorphization—suggests that the decisive physics is not only the magnitude of the transient heating but also the competition between rapid disordering and equally rapid recovery mechanisms that preserve fluorite-like order. It therefore raises a mechanism-level question that fixed-charge atomistic models cannot address cleanly: are redox flexibility and oxygen nonstoichiometry merely passive byproducts of the thermal spike, or do they actively feed back on vacancy and polaron energetics strongly enough to modify the emergent track morphology and early recovery?

Answering that question requires an atomistic model that remains accurate under extreme, non-equilibrium conditions and across a range of redox environments. Classical potentials with fixed oxidation states struggle in this setting because the local chemistry can evolve continuously from oxidized fluorite-like coordination to vacancy-rich motifs where $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ formation is essential. At the same time, direct DFT simulations cannot reach the length and time scales required to form and analyze latent tracks. The strategy adopted here is to build a redox-capable machine-learning interatomic potential (MLIP) whose reference is defined by a calibrated DFT+U description of Ce–O bonding and $4f$ localization. We first validate the DFT+U level against spectral and energetic signatures that are sensitive to redox chemistry—most directly the relative placement of $\mathrm{O}(2p)$, $\mathrm{Ce}(4f)$, and $\mathrm{Ce}(5d)$ features—then use that reference to generate a training corpus that explicitly spans the oxidized and reduced end members and representative intermediate oxygen-deficient phases ($\mathrm{CeO}_2$, $\mathrm{Ce}_{11}\mathrm{O}_{20}$, $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$, $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$; Fig. 1). This coverage ensures that the resulting MLIP is forced to learn the distinct local coordination motifs and energetic responses associated with $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}$-rich and $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-rich environments. With that potential in hand, we can then couple electronic stopping to atomistic dynamics through two-temperature molecular dynamics and test, in a controlled manner, whether redox and oxygen nonstoichiometry are spectators or active participants in SHI track formation and recovery.

# AIM

The overarching aim of this work is to enable predictive, large-scale atomistic simulations of swift heavy-ion (SHI) track formation in a redox-active fluorite oxide using a single, internally consistent modeling stack that connects electronic stopping to defect production. Ceria is an ideal test bed for this goal: it retains a mechanically robust fluorite lattice like actinide dioxides, but also supports oxygen nonstoichiometry and $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ redox chemistry that can be activated under the extreme, transient conditions of a thermal spike. Capturing this coupling is essential if one wishes to test whether track morphology and early recovery are governed purely by thermomechanical disordering or whether local reduction, vacancy formation, and polaron localization feed back on the energetics and kinetics that control damage accumulation.

A first objective is therefore to establish a DFT+U reference description that is faithful in precisely the degree of freedom that distinguishes oxidized and reduced ceria: the placement and localization of Ce($4f$) states relative to the $\mathrm{O}(2p)$ manifold. We calibrate and validate this reference level using directly interpretable diagnostics—spin polarization (as a proxy for $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ formation), density-of-states features, and band-alignment measures such as the $\mathrm{O}(2p)$–Ce($4f$)/Ce($5d$) separations—and cross-check the resulting trends against established benchmarks. This step fixes the electronic-structure “ground truth” that will be used to label training configurations and avoids the common failure mode in redox-active oxides where a potential is trained to reproduce forces while inadvertently inheriting an inconsistent or unphysical picture of $4f$ localization.

The second objective is to translate that calibrated reference into a training set that spans the relevant redox and structural subspaces accessed during SHI irradiation. Rather than treating $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ as a single fixed composition, we deliberately sample along the reduction series—from fluorite $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ through intermediate oxygen-deficient phases ($\mathrm{Ce}_{11}\mathrm{O}_{20}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$) to the reduced end member $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$ (Fig. 1)—so that the model sees the distinct coordination motifs and bonding responses associated with vacancy-rich and $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-rich environments. To make the model relevant for non-equilibrium spike conditions, the dataset is further broadened with strained structures and finite-temperature snapshots, ensuring that the training labels cover not only relaxed crystals but also anharmonic distortions and short-range disorder.

The final objective is to deploy the resulting redox-capable ML interatomic potential within a two-temperature molecular dynamics (TTM-MD) framework for SHI track simulations. In this multiscale setting, electronic stopping is represented as a localized energy source in an electronic temperature field that exchanges energy with an explicit atomistic lattice evolved by the MLIP. This coupling makes it possible to generate and quench a thermal spike while retaining atomistic resolution of defect production, oxygen sublattice rearrangement, and early recovery, thereby providing a controlled platform for assessing how redox flexibility and oxygen nonstoichiometry influence emergent track descriptors such as radius, underdensity, and crystalline retention.



# Redox-Capable Ce-O MLIP Training with DFT+U and Allegro


![[Pasted image 20260223092733.png|500]]


Swift-heavy-ion thermal spikes drive ceria far from equilibrium over picosecond time scales, sampling large-amplitude vibrational disorder, transient local densification/expansion, and oxygen sublattice rearrangements. In a redox-active oxide this disorder is coupled to changes in cerium valence: oxygen vacancy formation and migration can be accompanied by localization of excess electrons into $\mathrm{Ce}(4f)$-like polaronic states, and the local bonding environment around $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ differs measurably from that around $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}$. The core requirement for a “redox-capable” Ce–O ML interatomic potential (MLIP), therefore, is not an explicit charge variable but a reference dataset that forces the model to learn—through geometry and local coordination alone—the distinct energetic and force responses associated with oxidized, reduced, and intermediate oxygen-deficient environments.

We construct this reference from a calibrated DFT+U description of the Ce–O system and deliberately span the ceria reduction series with four representative compositions: $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ (fully oxidized fluorite), $\mathrm{Ce}_{11}\mathrm{O}_{20}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$ (intermediate reduced phases that contain substantial $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ fractions and ordered oxygen deficiency), and $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$ (the reduced end member). The same electronic-structure settings—including the chosen $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$ on Ce $4f$ states and spin polarization—are applied consistently across phases so that the training labels reflect a single, well-defined reference energy landscape rather than a mixture of incompatible approximations. Reduced phases are treated as intrinsically spin-polarized (consistent with localized $4f$ physics), and practical dataset generation must account for the fact that DFT+U can admit multiple local minima associated with different $4f$ occupations; in practice this means using controlled initial magnetic moments/occupations and verifying that relaxed and finite-temperature snapshots retain physically reasonable localization patterns rather than collapsing into artificially delocalized solutions.

Finite-temperature sampling is then used to broaden the dataset beyond harmonic distortions around ideal crystal structures. Starting from relaxed unit cells/supercells of each stoichiometry, we generate configurations by (i) applying small homogeneous strains (to teach the MLIP elastic responses needed for stress waves and thermomechanical coupling), (ii) introducing oxygen-sublattice disorder and vacancy-like motifs consistent with the targeted stoichiometries, and (iii) running ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) with DFT+U forces to sample thermally disordered snapshots at multiple temperatures spanning near-ambient conditions up toward the regime where the lattice becomes highly anharmonic. From these AIMD trajectories we export per-configuration labels—total energies and atomic forces, and where available the virial stress—so that the MLIP can be trained not only to reproduce equilibrium energetics but also to provide stable, accurate forces under the strongly non-equilibrium atomic environments encountered during spike-driven heating and quenching.

Because AIMD produces many nearly redundant configurations (adjacent frames can be almost identical), we curate the corpus before training. Near-duplicate structures are removed using geometric similarity criteria so that the model is not overfit to overrepresented regions of configuration space, and the remaining configurations are balanced across the four phases so that the potential does not become disproportionately specialized to the most frequently sampled composition (typically $\mathrm{CeO}_2$). Dataset splits are performed in a way that avoids leakage of strongly correlated frames between training and evaluation (e.g., by splitting at the trajectory or structural-prototype level rather than randomly frame-by-frame), yielding distinct training, validation, and test sets that genuinely probe transfer across temperatures, strains, and stoichiometries.

With these labels, we train an Allegro potential, which builds a local neighbor graph around each atom and learns rotationally equivariant features of the local environment to predict an atomic energy contribution. The total energy is written as a sum over atomic energies,
$$
E(\{\mathbf{r}_i\})=\sum_i E_i,
$$
and forces are obtained as the exact negative gradient of the predicted energy,
$$
\mathbf{F}_i=-\nabla_i E.
$$
Training minimizes a weighted objective that emphasizes force fidelity (crucial for stable, accurate MD) while still constraining relative energetics across phases and defects,
$$
\mathcal{L}=w_E\|\Delta E\|^2+w_F\|\Delta \mathbf{F}\|^2 \; (+\, w_\sigma\|\Delta \boldsymbol{\sigma}\|^2 \text{ when stress labels are included}),
$$
where the weights are tuned to keep energy and force errors commensurate for the intended MD use case. The practical outcome of this workflow is an MLIP that can interpolate across oxidized and reduced local environments—including oxygen-deficient motifs that imply $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ formation in the DFT+U reference—without requiring any explicit charge assignment during subsequent large-scale simulations.


# Swift Heavy Ion Track Simulations with TwoTemperature Molecular Dynamics



![[Screenshot 2026-02-23 at 9.28.21 AM.png|500]]


To connect the electronic-stopping physics of swift heavy ions (SHIs) to atomistic defect production, we use a two-temperature molecular dynamics (TTM-MD) framework in which the electronic subsystem is represented as a continuum field coupled to an explicit atomistic lattice. The SHI deposits energy primarily into electronic excitations along the ion trajectory; in TTM-MD this is introduced as a spatiotemporal source term, $S_e(\mathbf{r},t)$, acting on an electronic temperature field defined on a coarse mesh surrounding the ion path. In practice, $S_e$ is constructed to represent the experimental stopping power (energy deposited per unit path length) and is distributed radially about the track axis with a characteristic width that sets the initial radius of energy deposition; its time dependence is chosen to reflect the ultrafast character of the excitation relative to the subsequent picosecond lattice heating and quenching.

The coupled evolution of the electronic and lattice energy reservoirs is captured by the standard two-temperature equations. In a representative form,
$$
C_e(T_e)\,\frac{\partial T_e}{\partial t}
=\nabla\cdot\!\left(k_e(T_e)\nabla T_e\right)
-g\left(T_e-T_l\right)
+S_e(\mathbf{r},t),
$$
$$
C_l(T_l)\,\frac{\partial T_l}{\partial t}
=g\left(T_e-T_l\right),
$$
where $T_e(\mathbf{r},t)$ is the electronic temperature, $T_l(\mathbf{r},t)$ is the lattice temperature, $C_e$ and $C_l$ are the electronic and lattice heat capacities, $k_e$ is the electronic thermal conductivity, and $g$ is the electron–phonon coupling constant that controls the rate at which electronic energy is transferred to atomic motion. The key physical consequence of these equations is that the electronic excitation rapidly diffuses and relaxes while simultaneously heating the lattice locally, producing a transient “thermal spike” with large temperature gradients and strong anharmonic atomic motion near the ion path.

The atomistic MD region, evolved with the trained Ce–O MLIP, provides the microscopic pathway by which this transient heating produces defects, disorder, and local structural transformation. Coupling between the continuum temperatures and the MD atoms is implemented by mapping the mesh-resolved lattice temperature onto the atomic domain and applying a local, time-dependent thermostatting/exchange term that reproduces the electron–phonon energy flow implied by $g(T_e-T_l)$. Operationally, atoms are assigned to electronic/lattice mesh cells (or interpolated from nearby nodes), and the coupling term is applied as a Langevin-type friction and stochastic forcing whose net work transfers energy between the electronic field and the atomic kinetic energy in a manner consistent with the continuum equations. This approach preserves the essential separation of scales: electron energy transport and deposition are treated at the mesh level, while bond breaking, defect clustering, and short-range disordering/recovery are resolved explicitly by MD.

Finite-size effects are controlled by surrounding the active track-forming region with a dissipative boundary layer (“Langevin rim”) held near the ambient temperature $T_0$. With an appropriate damping coefficient $\gamma_L$, this rim acts as an energy and pressure-wave absorber, reducing unphysical reflections of heat and stress that would otherwise interfere with track formation and early recovery in a finite MD cell. After the spike and subsequent quench, track descriptors are extracted directly from the atomistic configurations—e.g., radial profiles of density and local order/disorder metrics (coordination changes, defect counts, crystallinity proxies)—to quantify track radius, underdensity, and the degree of crystalline retention versus disorder as functions of deposited energy and model parameters.



# RESULTS


![[Pasted image 20260223092942.png|500]]


![[Pasted image 20260223093005.png|500]]

The central purpose of the DFT+U calibration is to ensure that the reference electronic structure used to label the MLIP training set places the key Ce–O states in physically meaningful positions, especially the relative alignment of the $\mathrm{O}(2p)$ valence manifold with the Ce-derived $\mathrm{4f}$ and $\mathrm{5d}$ manifolds. These alignments control whether reduced configurations exhibit localized $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-like behavior (occupied $4f$ weight and nonzero spin polarization) and whether the oxidized end member remains a robust insulator with a clean separation between $\mathrm{O}(2p)$-dominated valence states and the lowest unoccupied Ce-derived states. In the diagnostics reported here, the “$2p$–$4f$” and “$2p$–$5d$” metrics summarize these alignments by measuring the separation between the $\mathrm{O}(2p)$ manifold and the dominant Ce($4f$) or Ce($5d$) feature used as the relevant near-gap reference for a given phase. The DOS panels and the accompanying magnetization/band-alignment summary therefore serve as the practical checks used to select and validate an appropriate $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$ window for dataset generation.

Across the scanned range ($U_{\mathrm{eff}}=4$–$6\ \mathrm{eV}$), $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ is comparatively stable. The DOS retains the expected fluorite-ceria character: a predominantly $\mathrm{O}(2p)$ valence band, an insulating gap, and unoccupied Ce $4f$ weight appearing as the lowest-lying Ce-derived feature above the Fermi level, with the broader Ce $5d$ manifold higher in energy. Consistent with this picture, the calculations remain non-magnetic (final magnetization $=0$ in the summary table), indicating that the oxidized state does not spuriously localize a $4f$ electron. Quantitatively, the $\mathrm{O}(2p)\rightarrow\mathrm{Ce}(4f)$ separation increases only modestly over this range (from $2.29\ \mathrm{eV}$ at $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=4\ \mathrm{eV}$ to $2.59\ \mathrm{eV}$ at $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=6\ \mathrm{eV}$), while the higher-energy $\mathrm{O}(2p)\rightarrow\mathrm{Ce}(5d)$ separation remains on the order of $\sim 5\ \mathrm{eV}$ and varies weakly with $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$ in this window. This weak sensitivity in the oxidized end member is helpful for MLIP reference building because the $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ labels are not overly dependent on fine tuning of the Hubbard correction.

Reduced compositions, by contrast, exhibit the strong correlation signatures that motivate DFT+U. Both $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$ are strongly spin-polarized, consistent with localized $4f^1$ electrons on $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ sites, and both show prominent Ce($4f$) weight in the near-gap region of the DOS. The final magnetizations reported for $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$ are $\approx 1.9\ \mu_B$ over $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=4$–$6\ \mathrm{eV}$, close to the $2\ \mu_B$ expected for two $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ ions per formula unit (each contributing one unpaired $4f$ electron). For $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$, the net moment is $\approx 3.8\ \mu_B$, consistent with its mixed-valence stoichiometry: interpreting $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$ as $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ gives $x=2-12/7\approx 0.286$ and an expected $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ fraction $\sim 2x\approx 0.57$, i.e., roughly four $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ ions per seven cerium atoms and a corresponding $\sim 4\ \mu_B$ moment. In these reduced and vacancy-rich environments, the position and splitting of $4f$ features—and therefore metrics built from $\mathrm{O}(2p)$–Ce($4f$) and $\mathrm{O}(2p)$–Ce($5d$) separations—vary more noticeably with $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$ than in $\mathrm{CeO}_2$, underscoring that the Hubbard parameter matters most precisely where redox chemistry changes the near-gap electronic structure.

This stronger sensitivity is visible in the numerical diagnostics. For $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$, the reported $2p$–$4f$ separation decreases from $3.6\ \mathrm{eV}$ at $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=4\ \mathrm{eV}$ to $1.76\ \mathrm{eV}$ at $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=6\ \mathrm{eV}$, reflecting a substantial shift of the dominant Ce($4f$) feature relative to the $\mathrm{O}(2p)$ manifold as the Hubbard correction is increased. The $2p$–$5d$ separation is smaller in this reduced end member and collapses toward zero by $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=6\ \mathrm{eV}$ in the reported summary, highlighting how band ordering and near-gap alignment in $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-rich environments can change qualitatively with $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$ if the electronic structure is not carefully benchmarked. For $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$, the net moment remains near $\sim 4\ \mu_B$ across the scan (the sign flip at $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=4\ \mathrm{eV}$ simply reflects an arbitrary choice of spin quantization), while the $2p$–$5d$ separation stays in the $3.76$–$3.9\ \mathrm{eV}$ range and the $2p$–$4f$ metric varies non-monotonically across $U_{\mathrm{eff}}=4$–$6\ \mathrm{eV}$. Taken at face value, these trends emphasize that intermediate stoichiometries can combine robust magnetic moments (indicating persistent $4f$ localization) with $U$-dependent near-gap alignments that are sensitive to details of how mixed-valence and vacancy ordering are represented.

Literature benchmarks mirror this hierarchy of sensitivity. Löschen et al. systematically mapped how relevant band separations evolve with $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$ in $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$ (Phys. Rev. B 75, 035115 (2007); plotted above), showing that the placement of Ce $4f$ features—and properties derived from them—can vary substantially with the Hubbard correction, especially in reduced ceria where $4f$ localization is essential to obtaining the correct insulating and magnetic character. Taken together with the DOS and magnetization diagnostics reported here, this motivates a conservative strategy for MLIP reference generation: the training set must explicitly include reduced stoichiometries and oxygen-deficient local environments (not just $\mathrm{CeO}_2$) and must include configurations that probe $4f$ localization, because the largest $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$-driven variations occur precisely where redox chemistry changes the bonding and near-gap electronic structure that governs vacancy energetics and early recovery during thermal-spike quenching.


# Oxygen Vacancies and Reduction in CeO2-x
Oxygen vacancies provide the simplest chemical coordinate that connects lattice disorder to redox chemistry in ceria. It is convenient to write the reduced material as $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$, where $x$ denotes the oxygen deficiency per cerium relative to stoichiometric fluorite $\mathrm{CeO}_2$. In this notation, removing an oxygen atom from the anion sublattice creates an oxygen vacancy and leaves behind two electrons. In a physically faithful description of reduced ceria, those electrons do not remain delocalized in the conduction manifold; instead they localize on nearby Ce sites, converting $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}$ into $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-like centers with occupied $4f$ character (small-polaron localization in the DFT+U reference). A compact way to express this bookkeeping is through charge neutrality. For $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$, the total negative charge per Ce is $-2(2-x)=-(4-2x)$, so the average cerium valence must be
$$
\overline{v}_{\mathrm{Ce}} = 4-2x.
$$
Equivalently, if one interprets the material as a mixture of $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$, the average $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ fraction is $f_{\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}}\approx 2x$ (bounded by $0\le x\le 0.5$ over the fluorite-to-sesquioxide reduction series). This linear relation makes clear why vacancy-rich configurations are also the most correlation sensitive: each vacancy increases the local concentration of $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-like environments that carry localized $4f$ electrons and spin polarization.

The same stoichiometric parameter also provides a natural measure of vacancy concentration. In the ideal fluorite lattice, there are two oxygen sites per cerium; therefore the fraction of oxygen sites that are vacant is
$$
f_{v_\mathrm{O}}=\frac{x}{2}.
$$
The reduction-series compositions used throughout this work map cleanly onto this scale. $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ corresponds to $x=0$ and $f_{v_\mathrm{O}}=0$, while the reduced end member $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$ corresponds to $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.5}$, i.e., $x=0.5$ and $f_{v_\mathrm{O}}=0.25$ (one quarter of the fluorite oxygen sites vacant). The intermediate phases shown in Fig. 1 are conveniently expressed in the same language: $\mathrm{Ce}_{11}\mathrm{O}_{20}$ has $\mathrm{O}/\mathrm{Ce}=20/11\approx 1.818$, so $x=2-20/11\approx 0.182$ and $f_{v_\mathrm{O}}\approx 0.091$; $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$ has $\mathrm{O}/\mathrm{Ce}=12/7\approx 1.714$, so $x\approx 0.286$ and $f_{v_\mathrm{O}}\approx 0.143$. The schematic vacancy patterns in the figure visualize this progression: as $x$ increases, more oxygen sites are removed and the material necessarily contains a higher density of $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-rich local coordination environments.

For SHI thermal-spike modeling, these relations are more than stoichiometric bookkeeping. The spike can transiently disorder the oxygen sublattice and change local density, both of which can alter vacancy formation and migration barriers; in a redox-capable reference, such structural changes are coupled to whether electrons localize as $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ polarons. By explicitly including configurations drawn from $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ through vacancy-rich intermediates to $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$, the DFT+U dataset and the trained MLIP are forced to represent the continuous spectrum of local environments implied by $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$—from fully oxidized fluorite-like neighborhoods to strongly reduced, vacancy-dominated motifs—so that subsequent TTM-MD simulations can interrogate how oxygen deficiency and redox flexibility feed back on defect production and early recovery within the latent track.

# CONCLUSION
This chapter establishes a consistent electronic-structure reference and a corresponding data-generation strategy for redox-capable Ce–O machine-learning interatomic potentials aimed at swift-heavy-ion (SHI) track simulations. By scanning $\mathrm{U}_{\mathrm{eff}}=4$–$6\ \mathrm{eV}$ across oxidized and reduced compositions—$\mathrm{CeO}_2$, $\mathrm{Ce}_7\mathrm{O}_{12}$, and $\mathrm{Ce}_2\mathrm{O}_3$—and interpreting the resulting DOS, magnetization, and band-alignment diagnostics in the context of established benchmarks (e.g., Löschen et al., Phys. Rev. B 75, 035115 (2007)), we define a DFT+U parameter window that preserves the essential qualitative physics required for redox modeling: $\mathrm{CeO}_2$ remains a robust non-magnetic insulator with well-separated $\mathrm{O}(2p)$ and Ce-derived unoccupied states, while reduced phases exhibit the expected spin polarization and near-gap Ce($4f$) character associated with $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ formation.

The results also make clear where the reference description is most sensitive and therefore where the MLIP must be most carefully constrained. Variation with $\mathrm{U}_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is comparatively modest in $\mathrm{CeO}_2$, but becomes pronounced in reduced and vacancy-rich chemistries where $4f$ localization and near-gap alignments evolve strongly with the Hubbard correction. Because SHI thermal spikes transiently disorder the oxygen sublattice and can drive local oxygen deficiency, the atomistic model cannot be trained only on the oxidized end member without risking systematic errors precisely in the environments that dominate track formation and early recovery.

For that reason, the $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$ calibration is converted directly into concrete dataset requirements for the Allegro MLIP: the training corpus must span the oxidized and reduced stoichiometries along the $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ reduction series, explicitly include oxygen-vacancy and $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-rich local environments, and incorporate strained as well as high-temperature disordered snapshots so that the same $4f$-driven physics diagnosed in the electronic structure is exercised under the large stresses, temperature gradients, and anharmonic motion characteristic of SHI-induced thermal spikes. With this foundation in place, subsequent two-temperature-driven MD can use a single, internally consistent potential to test how redox flexibility and oxygen nonstoichiometry modulate defect production, crystalline retention, and the emergent morphology of latent tracks in ceria-based materials.
