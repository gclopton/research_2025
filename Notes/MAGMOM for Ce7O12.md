On the structure itself and the Ce sublattices: Ce₇O₁₂ in the ($R\bar{3}$) structure **has two inequivalent Ce sites, usually called Ce(1) and Ce(2)**. Neutron diffraction and bond-valence analysis show that Ce(2) is seven-coordinate and somewhat more reduced, while Ce(1) is six-coordinate and more oxidized.([ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Identification-of-the-coordination-polyhedra-in-Ce7O12-for-bond-valence-calculations_fig3_43512139?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Identification of the coordination polyhedra in Ce7O12 for ...")) So at the level of “Ce³⁺-rich vs Ce⁴⁺-rich,” Ce(2) is your Ce³⁺-leaning, seven-coordinate sublattice, and Ce(1) is your Ce⁴⁺-leaning, six-coordinate sublattice.

You can see this manually from the special coordinates, as I just did, but you can also do it rigorously with a Python script. Mathematically, the idea is simple. Each site is given by a fractional coordinate vector (f_i = (f_x, f_y, f_z)); the Cartesian position is (R_i = L f_i), where (L) is the 3×3 lattice matrix built from your three lattice vectors. For each Ce site (i) and each O site (j), you consider all lattice translations (n \in \mathbb{Z}^3) and define the minimum-image displacement  
[  
\Delta R_{ij} = L (f_j + n - f_i),  
]  
choosing the integer vector (n) that minimizes (|\Delta R_{ij}|). The Ce–O distance is then (d_{ij} = |\Delta R_{ij}|). If you pick a cutoff between the first and second Ce–O shells, say (r_\text{cut} \approx 2.9) Å (the first shell in Ce₇O₁₂ lies roughly between 2.28 and 2.62 Å),([Materials Project](https://next-gen.materialsproject.org/materials/mp-2629?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Materials Explorer")) the coordination number of Ce site (i) is just the count of O neighbors with (d_{ij} < r_\text{cut}). When you apply that to your cell, Ce 7, 14 and 21 come out with coordination 6; the rest come out with coordination 7. A small pymatgen or ASE script would implement exactly that logic under the hood.

For this cell, doing it “by eye” is manageable because the Ce(1) sites sit at obvious rational coordinates, but I would still recommend a script: it generalises to other supercells and gives you an unambiguous mapping from POSCAR indices to Ce(1)/Ce(2) without guesswork. Conceptually, though, the mathematics is just “compute distances with the minimum-image convention and count neighbors within a cutoff.”



On the magnitude side, your question “should I try just +1 and +2?” is basically yes. A Ce³⁺ ion with an ($f^1$) configuration has (S = 1/2), so the spin-only moment is about 1 µB. In PBE+U simulations of reduced ceria, converged Ce³⁺ local moments almost always fall near that value, while Ce⁴⁺ sites are essentially non-magnetic.([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/0910.0669?utm_source=chatgpt.com "A Consistent Picture of the Charge Distribution in Reduced ...")) So you don’t need to seed 5 µB; that was a useful “big kick” for a first blind run, but it is not a good production default. For a systematic check, it is perfectly adequate to do one run with all Ce(2) at +1.0 µB and Ce(1) at 0, and a second run with Ce(2) at +2.0 µB and Ce(1) at 0, keeping all O at 0 in both cases. If the SCF converges in both cases to essentially the same set of local moments and nearly identical total energies, you have strong evidence that your results are insensitive to the precise initial magnitude and that 1 µB is a sensible default. You do not need a finer “grid” of magnitudes beyond that.



About the “vacancy” wording and whether your current cell has any: relative to stoichiometric fluorite CeO₂, Ce₇O₁₂ is oxygen-deficient. If you had 21 Ce in perfect CeO₂, you would have 42 O; in this structure you only have 36 O, so there are six missing oxygens per cell, or one vacancy per Ce₇O₁₂ formula unit.([OSTI](https://www.osti.gov/pages/biblio/1797516?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Review of Swift Heavy Ion Irradiation Effects in CeO2")) You did not manually delete any O, but the Materials Project structure you started from already encodes a specific ordered pattern of oxygen vacancies. When I say “Ce around a vacancy,” I mean the cluster of Ce(2) sites that are nearest neighbors to one of those missing O sites in the ordered Ce₇O₁₂ structure.

For your MAGMOM experiments, though, you do not have to micro-resolve each vacancy cluster to get reasonable seeds. The more important question is the global spin pattern: do the Ce(2) moments all start parallel (ferromagnetic-like), or do you seed some up and some down (antiferromagnetic-like) in a way that roughly respects the symmetry? Also, you do not need both “all up” and “all down” runs; a global flip of all spins leaves the Hamiltonian unchanged in collinear PBE+U, so those two seeds are exactly degenerate and collapse to the same physical solution. One ferromagnetic-like seed where all Ce(2) moments are, say, +1 µB is sufficient.

If you want to probe an antiferromagnetic-like pattern, a simple scheme is to split the 18 Ce(2) sites into two sets of nine and assign +1 µB to one set and −1 µB to the other, keeping all Ce(1) and O sites at 0. A more physically motivated way to choose those two sets is to group Ce(2) atoms by layer along the hexagonal (c)-direction or by their proximity to a given oxygen vacancy; you can do that by looking at the fractional (z) coordinates or with a script that clusters Ce(2) sites around the missing O positions. But even a coarse “half up, half down” split on the Ce(2) sublattice will usually relax towards whatever AFM arrangement, if any, is actually energetically favorable. The key point is that you are exploring different relative orientations on the Ce(2) sublattice, not separately testing “all up” and “all down.”



For the “two sublattices” and how to encode them in MAGMOM for this exact POSCAR: as noted above, Ce atoms 7, 14 and 21 (the ones at (0,0,0), (2/3,1/3,1/3) and (1/3,2/3,2/3)) form the Ce(1) six-coordinate sublattice. All the other Ce lines are Ce(2), the seven-coordinate sublattice. If you want a ferromagnetic Ce(2) seed with 1 µB, you can exploit VASP’s repetition syntax while respecting the existing order of atoms. In your current Ce ordering, the pattern is “six Ce(2), one Ce(1), six Ce(2), one Ce(1), six Ce(2), one Ce(1).” That means you can write a compact line like

`MAGMOM = 6*1.0 0.0 6*1.0 0.0 6*1.0 0.0 36*0.0`

which assigns 1 µB to each Ce(2), 0 to each Ce(1), and 0 to all 36 O. For a 2 µB variant you would just change the three “1.0” to “2.0”. For an AFM variant you would have to break the repetition pattern and explicitly list some Ce(2) as +1 and some as −1, but you still keep all Ce(1) at 0. Once you see that Ce(1) are exactly sites 7, 14 and 21, it becomes a bookkeeping problem rather than a mystery.



On whether a MAGMOM “sweep” is necessary at all: in this system we already know quite a lot from chemistry and prior electronic-structure work. The seven-coordinate Ce(2) sites are more reduced (closer to Ce³⁺), the six-coordinate Ce(1) are more oxidized (closer to Ce⁴⁺), and DFT+U tends to give Ce³⁺ local moments of order 1 µB.([Electronics and Books](https://electronicsandbooks.com/edt/manual/Magazine/J/Journal%20of%20Physics%20Condensed%20Matter/2010%20Volume%2022/0953-8984_22_22_223201.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Charge distribution near bulk oxygen vacancies in cerium ...")) That strongly suggests that the “most probable ideal” pattern is exactly what we just described: Ce(2) seeded at about 1 µB, Ce(1) and O at 0. The only reason to do a small set of alternative seeds (e.g. 2 µB on Ce(2), or an AFM-like arrangement) is not because we are ignorant of the likely physics, but to verify that your converged solution is robust and not an artefact of a particular initial guess. So yes, the massive MAGMOM grid you were worried about is not necessary; a handful of chemically motivated seeds is enough.



Finally, on whether a single spin-polarized PBE+U single-point is enough to diagnose all of this: for the questions we’re discussing—“are my Ce(2) sites carrying ~1 µB?”, “are Ce(1) nearly non-magnetic?”, “does a different MAGMOM seed change the final pattern?”—a static calculation on a fixed geometry is perfectly fine. The distribution of f-electron spin density is determined by the electronic structure; relaxing the ionic positions will refine the Ce–O bond lengths and total energy, but it rarely flips a clearly Ce³⁺ site into Ce⁴⁺ or vice versa in a well-ordered phase like Ce₇O₁₂. So you can absolutely use your existing TEST7 run, and maybe one or two additional static runs with alternative MAGMOM seeds, to decide on a sensible default MAGMOM pattern and to confirm that the Ce³⁺/Ce⁴⁺ picture you expect is what PBE+U is actually producing. When you later do fully relaxed calculations, you can reuse that same MAGMOM setup with confidence.
