# Abstract

The electronic structure and properties of cerium oxides ( $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ ) have been studied in the framework of the LDA+U and GGA(PW91)+U implementations of density functional theory. The dependence of selected observables of these materials on the effective U parameter has been investigated in detail. The examined properties include lattice constants, bulk moduli, density of states, and formation energies of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$. For $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$, the LDA +U results are in better agreement with experiment than the GGA+U results whereas for the computationally more demanding $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ both approaches give comparable accuracy. Furthermore, as expected, $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ is much more sensitive to the choice of the U value. Generally, the PW91 functional provides an optimal agreement with experiment at lower U energies than LDA does. In order to achieve a balanced description of both kinds of materials, and also of nonstoichiometric $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ phases, an appropriate choice of U is suggested for $\mathrm{LDA}+\mathrm{U}$ and GGA +U schemes. Nevertheless, an optimum value appears to be property dependent, especially for $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$. Optimum U values are found to be, in general, larger than values determined previously in a self-consistent way.

# I. INTRODUCTION

Cerium dioxide (ceria) is a component of important catalytic applications such as the 3-way car exhaust catalysis, $\mathrm{SO}_{x}$ reduction and the water gas shift reaction. ${ }^{1,2}$ There is evidence that ceria does not only function as a support for different precious metals. Rather, due to its facile reducibility, ceria can also directly participate in chemical reactions. Its activity is ascribed to the oxygen storage capacity, ${ }^{1}$ which takes place according to the formal reaction scheme: 2 Ce (IV) $\mathrm{O}_{2} \rightarrow \mathrm{Ce}_{2}$ (III) $\mathrm{O}_{3}+0.5 \mathrm{O}_{2}$. Thus, the equally accurate theoretical description of pure and mixed Ce (III) and Ce (IV) oxide materials is of primary importance for understanding their functioning and advancement of their practical applications. However, this is not a simple task since the additional electron, which is introduced on the Ce center upon reduction of Ce (IV) to Ce (III), is a strongly correlated $f$ electron. Hence, from a computational point of view, a balanced theoretical description of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ oxides is not straightforward. The challenge arises when one aims at a unified treatment of both oxidation states as has been addressed recently by a number of theoretical studies of bulk ceria $^{3-8}$ and (defective) ceria surfaces. ${ }^{6,9-14}$

In principle, the computational description of the insulating $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ within conventional density functional theory (DFT) is more or less straightforward due to the unoccupied $\mathrm{Ce}(4 f)$ state. The band gap is still underestimated but this is a well-known deficiency of standard DFT, in particular in the description of transition metal oxides such as $\mathrm{NiO} .^{15-18}$ ([15](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.30.4734>), [16](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.44.3604>), [17](<https://doi.org/10.1016/S0009-2614(01)00616-9>), [18](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.65.155102>)) Nevertheless, most of the recent DFT calculations yield a description of this material in close accordance with experimental findings, quite independent of the exchange correlation functionals employed. On the contrary, the $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ insulator has been known to be a notorious problem case for electronic structure calculations. From the literature published since this paper, it is now well established that calculations using the standard local density approach (LDA) or generalized gradient approach (GGA) frequently yield an incorrect metallic ground state for $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ unless one goes beyond semilocal DFT, most commonly via DFT+$\mathrm{U}$ or screened hybrid functionals; methods such as GW@LDA+$\mathrm{U}$ and DFT+DMFT have also been applied to cerium oxides when more faithful $f$-electron physics and spectra are needed. ${ }^{58-61}$ ([58](<https://doi.org/10.1021/jacsau.5c00095>), [59](<https://doi.org/10.1088/0953-8984/24/7/075604>), [60](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevLett.102.126403>), [61](<https://doi.org/10.1002/qua.24521>)) The use of ultrasoft pseudopotentials may give the right insulating solution but it is likely that this is due to a fortuitous error cancellation. ${ }^{4,5}$ ([4](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.71.041102>), [5](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.72.237101>)) An accurate calculation demands a modification of the usually employed DFT approaches to account for the strong localization of the Ce $f$ electron in the formal Ce oxidation state III.

The shortcomings of standard DFT may be overcome by different approaches, for example by correction of the self-interaction of electrons (==self-interaction correction, SIC==), which has been used for cerium oxides ${ }^{19,20}$ and related systems. ${ }^{21}$ A DFT hybrid approach has also been shown to give improved results for the electronic structure of Ce (III) oxide [Method 1]. ${ }^{7}$ Another promising way to improve contemporary LDA and GGA approaches is to modify the intra-atomic Coulomb interaction through the LDA+U approach. ${ }^{22-24}$ Indeed, this approach allows for a correct treatment of the electronic state of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ as has already been shown by de Fabris et al [method 2?]. ${ }^{4}$ The physical idea behind the LDA+U or GGA+U schemes comes from the Hubbard Hamiltonian. In the practical implementations, the on-site two-electron integrals, which would appear in Hartree-Fock (or post Hartree-Fock) like treatment, are expressed in terms of two parameters. These are the Hubbard parameter U, which reflects the strength of the on-site Coulomb interaction, and the parameter $J$, which adjusts the strength of the exchange interaction. In the somewhat simplified, yet rotationally invariant method of Dudarev et al [is this a third method? or part of method 2?]. ${ }^{25}$ these two parameters are combined into a single parameter $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=\mathrm{U}-J$. This approach can be understood as the introduction of a penalty function which disfavors noninteger occupation numbers of the on-site density matrix. In short, DFT +U correction acts to reduce the one-electron potential locally for the specified orbitals of the respective atoms (e.g., Ce $f$ orbitals), therefore reducing the hybridization with orbitals of the ligands (e.g., O atoms); the $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=0$ case representing the DFT limit.

In general, the introduction of the effective parameter $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ leads to a drastic improvement in description of reduced ceria. ${ }^{4,5}$ However, the specific value of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ remains somewhat context- and property-dependent because its choice can strongly influence calculated observables, and practical DFT+$\mathrm{U}$ results can depend on implementation details such as the chosen projectors. ${ }^{58,61}$ ([58](<https://doi.org/10.1021/jacsau.5c00095>), [61](<https://doi.org/10.1002/qua.24521>)) From an $a b$ initio point of view, it would be desirable to determine this parameter in a self-consistent way, which has been done for $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ with the approach of Cococcioni et al. ${ }^{26}$ ([26](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.71.035105>)) resulting in $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=2.5-3.5 \mathrm{eV}$ and $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=1.5-2.0 \mathrm{eV}$ for the LDA +U and GGA(PBE) +U choices of the exchange-correlation functional, respectively. ${ }^{4}$ However, the use of a higher value of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ has yielded better agreement with experiment. Values of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=5 \mathrm{eV}$ and $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=6.3 \mathrm{eV}$ for the PW91 (Ref. 13) and PBE (Ref. 6) functionals, respectively, have been suggested. Additionally, the $\mathrm{DFT}+\mathrm{U}$ approach seems also to be dependent on the specific chosen projector functions. ${ }^{4}$ Therefore, a more practically oriented approach consists in taking $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ as a sort of empirical parameter and fitting its value to reproduce certain experimental observables much in the same way as it has been done in molecular quantum chemistry for the so-called hybrid functionals ${ }^{27}$ but avoiding the introduction of nonlocal exchange and its concomitant computational complications. Although some work in this direction has been published rather recently, i.e., the determination of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ in an empirical way for $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ with the PBE functional, ${ }^{6}$ subsequent benchmark studies and reviews have expanded substantially; they also make clear that first-principles strategies for estimating $\mathrm{U}$ (e.g., linear-response and DFPT-based approaches) are available, but the resulting effective $\mathrm{U}$ can remain functional-, projector-, and property-dependent, so empirical calibration or property-targeted validation remains common in ceria modeling. ${ }^{58,61,62}$ ([58](<https://doi.org/10.1021/jacsau.5c00095>), [61](<https://doi.org/10.1002/qua.24521>), [62](<https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevB.101.064305>)) [[First-principles LDA+U and GGA+U study of cerium oxides; Dependence on the effective U parameter–dated claims#Systematic study of U_eff]] In particular, $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ should also be included in the set of benchmarks when probing $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$, specially if one is interested in more problematic cases involving Ce (III), such as defective ceria, ceria nanoparticles, and surface redox reactions. Therefore, one may argue that only simultaneous consideration of various properties of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ will allow for a unified treatment of both main oxidation states of ceria, which is especially important for charge transfer chemical reactions involving a transition between Ce (III) and Ce (IV) and playing a fundamental role in the catalytic applications of these materials. ${ }^{1}$ Furthermore, at the time of this work, the optimal $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ parameter appeared to be strongly dependent on the particular exchange-correlation choice and systematic comparisons across common functional+$\mathrm{U}$ combinations were still limited; since then, a substantial literature has explored these sensitivities across bulk, surfaces, and defect/redox energetics. ${ }^{58}$ ([58](<https://doi.org/10.1021/jacsau.5c00095>)) This work aims at such a systematic study. We will provide evidence that a semiempirical fitting of the $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ parameter will allow for a significant improvement of the cerium oxide properties under investigation, and that the inclusion of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ in this study is vital because of its high sensitivity towards a change of the effective on-site Coulomb repulsion.

The conclusion to be drawn from this systematic study is that an additional Hubbard-type on-site electron interaction only modestly influences the structural properties of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$. For different $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ values under scrutiny deviations from experiment still lie within the accuracy usually achieved with standard LDA and GGA approaches. The influence of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ on the DOS is basically restricted to the empty $f$ band and, thus, does not qualitatively change the electronic distribution. Generally speaking, $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ can be handled fairly well within contemporary density functional methods and quite independently of the particular approach chosen. This is also evident from Table I and the numerous theoretical publications on cerium dioxide cited above.

## B. Atomic and electronic structure of $\mathbf{C e}_{\mathbf{2}} \mathbf{O}_{\mathbf{3}}$

Let us now turn to the lattice parameter $a_{0}$ of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ as predicted by the different DFT approaches (Table II). Very similarly to results discussed for $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ in Secs. III A, LDA slightly underestimates $a_{0}$ whereas GGA exhibits the opposite trend and overestimates it. The lattice parameter and the bulk modulus of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ as a function of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ are explicitly shown in Fig. 3. Introducing U into the LDA methods results in an increase of $a_{0}$ so that eventually LDA +U may predict the experimental lattice constant. However, since the GGA predicted $a_{0}$ is already too big, introduction of U into the GGA potential leads to an even worse estimate. To the best of our knowledge, the bulk modulus of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ has not been measured so far, but in analogy to the calculated trends reported above for $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ one may expect a value around 150 GPa .

Overall, the effect of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ is similar to what is found for $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$, but, and this is a crucial point, the observables of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ are much more affected by variation of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ than those of the nonmagnetic $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$. For $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ increasing from 0 to 9 eV , the lattice constant of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ varies over a range of $0.12 \AA$ and the bulk modulus changes over more than 40 GPa , compared to the corresponding variations of $0.06 \AA$ and 20 GPa for $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$. These data clearly demonstrate the
importance of including in the determination of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ also $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ as a substance considerably more sensitive to this parameter. Note that the unsteadiness of the bulk modulus [Fig. 3(b)], especially for small values of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$, is due to the fact that it is not possible to get a well-balanced energy versus volume curve in the area of small volumes where electronic solutions with localised $f$ electrons could not be found. However, this is not a problem for the lattice parameter as for its determination only a few values around the minimum of the energy versus volume curve are needed.

It is important to mention that the just discussed observables are computed for the ground state of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$, which we found to be insulating and antiferromagnetic, and which lies $25 \mathrm{meV}\left(\mathrm{LDA}+\mathrm{U}, \mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=5 \mathrm{eV}\right)$ and $24 \mathrm{meV}\left(\mathrm{GGA}+\mathrm{U}, \mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}\right. =3 \mathrm{eV}$ ) below the insulating ferromagnetic state. In a simple mapping approach ${ }^{47-51}$ this gives a Heisenberg exchange coupling constant $J \approx-50 \mathrm{meV}$. Unfortunately it is very difficult to assess the accuracy of this prediction since, to the best of our knowledge, no experimentally determined $J$ value for $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ has been reported. The presently computed value is significantly larger than the coupling constant of -6 meV predicted by Hay et al. ${ }^{7}$ using the HSE hybrid functional. In any case it is worth pointing out that LDA and GGA approaches tend to overestimate the Heisenberg exchange effective parameter ${ }^{49}$ and hybrid functionals exhibit very strong dependence on the amount of Fock exchange. ${ }^{52}$ One may also argue that the LDA+U or GGA+U values also depend on the choice of the $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ parameter. We have investigated the variation of $J$ with respect to $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ and found that this is rather small, within the range of a few meV. Moreover, a rather stable value of $J \approx-50 \mathrm{meV}$ is predicted for $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ in the $4-5 \mathrm{eV}$ range for the LDA+U and in the $2-3 \mathrm{eV}$ range for the GGA+U, respectively. The low influence of the effective on-site Coulomb repulsion on the Heisenberg exchange coupling can be rationalized by the fact, that in the LDA + U approach only one-center two-electron interactions are modified. This contrasts with the behavior of hybrid density functional approaches where a modification of the exchange potential strongly influences the electron interactions between different centers. ${ }^{49-52}$ Further studies dealing with the interplay between $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ and the exchange coupling for other systems than ceria are in preparation.

With respect to the electronic structure of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$, the major effect of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ in the LDA+U and GGA+U approaches is to force the localisation of the $4 f$ electron within the band gap. As a result, this material becomes to be properly described as insulating instead of metallic as has been discussed in detail by other groups. ${ }^{4,5}$ In the following we will focus on the changes that occur to the DOS when the on-site Coulomb interaction is modified via $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$.

## C. Reaction energies

In addition to the dependence of geometric and electronic structure observables on $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ we have also investigated the effect of this parameter on selected reaction energies involving ceria. First, we computed the formation energies of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ at zero pressure and zero temperature (see Fig. 6). The corresponding experimental values are -11.29 eV $\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\right)$ and $-18.63 \mathrm{eV}\left(\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}\right){ }^{54,55}$ To correctly evaluate these energies it is necessary to consider the $\gamma$ modification of fcc Ce at different values of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff. }}{ }^{56}$ (The results for bulk Ce are not reported here but are available from the authors upon request.) The reliability of the LSDA +U and the GGA+U schemes for fcc $\gamma$ cerium have been established by Shick et al. ${ }^{57}$ The minimum appearing in both curves is an artifact because for LDA+U with $U_{\text {eff }}<3 \mathrm{eV}$ and GGA+U with $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}<2 \mathrm{eV}$, the electron solutions for the $\gamma$-Ce reference collapsed to the high pressure solution for $\alpha$-Ce with itinerant $f$ electrons. It was therefore not possible to get the correct stable electronic solution for $\gamma$-Ce at these points. For sake of consistency one should then only consider reaction energies starting from $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=3 \mathrm{eV}$ or $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}=2 \mathrm{eV}$ for the LDA+U and GGA+U methods, respectively.

# IV. CONCLUSIONS

The bulk cerium oxides $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ have been studied by means of periodic density functional calculations including explicitly the Hubbard on-site Coulomb repulsion $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ parameter. The impact of this parameter on several structural and electronic observables of both materials has been investigated in a systematic way. It is found that the optimal value of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ is considerably dependent on the property under examination, and the sensitivity towards $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ is especially high for properties of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$. Overall, the scheme GGA(PW91) reveals an acceptable agreement with experiment at lower energies of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ than LDA does; this is partly due to the more accurate treatment of correlation effects within the GGA potential. Structural properties such as lattice constants and bulk modulus are somewhat better rep-
resented by the LDA+U method for $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$. Regarding $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ electronic structure, both LDA+U and GGA+U results show a similarly good accuracy. Interestingly, for the calculated reaction energies LDA+U scheme appears to perform better, although presumably taking benefit of error cancellation. The GGA(PW91) +U estimated reaction energies involving the molecular oxygen total energy may be improved by the correction as indicated above.

A well balanced choice of $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ is proposed to be at $5-6 \mathrm{eV}$ for the LDA+U calculations and at $2-3 \mathrm{eV}$ for the GGA + U ones. An important conclusion is that, in order to correctly reproduce the experimental observables, the Coulomb on-site repulsion has to be increased compared to the one determined in a self-consistent way ( $2.5-3.5 \mathrm{eV}$ for $\mathrm{LDA}+\mathrm{U}$ and $1.5-2.0 \mathrm{eV}$ for GGA +U ), although the physical reason for this remains unclear. We would like to point out that self-consistency on $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$, albeit a convenient conceptual procedure, is not supported from any fundamental theorem. In addition, it is well possible that screening effects due to other electrons are slightly overemphasized in the $\mathrm{DFT}+\mathrm{U}$ approaches.

Finally, it should be mentioned that even if this work is based on purely Ce (IV) or purely Ce (III) materials, the present conclusions are expected to be valid for other related materials of a mixed valence character, simultaneously containing Ce ions in the two oxidations states, such as defective ceria surfaces or nanosized ceria particles. In this sense, the suggested choice of an optimal $\mathrm{U}_{\text {eff }}$ parameter based on comparison to a large enough set of experimental data validates a promising and efficient way to study these systems within the $\mathrm{DFT}+\mathrm{U}$ formalism.
