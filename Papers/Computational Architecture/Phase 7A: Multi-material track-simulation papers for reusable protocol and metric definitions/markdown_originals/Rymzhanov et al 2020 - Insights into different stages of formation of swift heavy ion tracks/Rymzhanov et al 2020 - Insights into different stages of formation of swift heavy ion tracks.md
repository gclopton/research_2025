#### Abstract

This review summarizes our results on effects of different stages of the formation kinetics of swift heavy ion (SHI) tracks on final structural changes. The developed multiscale approach describes in detail the formation of individual tracks and inter-track interaction in various amorphizable $\left(\mathrm{Y}_{3} \mathrm{Al}_{5} \mathrm{O}_{12}, \mathrm{Mg}_{2} \mathrm{SiO}_{4}\right)$ and non-amorphizable $\left(\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}, \mathrm{MgO}\right)$ dielectrics. A comparison between the modeling and high-resolution transmission electron microscopy analysis of SHI irradiated samples allows to validate the developed model and to investigate links between the basic properties of the materials and features of the kinetics of structural transformations of the targets. We outline an influence and importance of each successive stage of excitation and relaxation on final observable structure modifications within tracks, starting from the SHI induced electronic excitation, electrons and holes transport, energy exchange with the target lattice, transient excitation and relaxation of the atomic system, and ultimate damage formation.


## 1. Introduction

A swift heavy ion (SHI) is typically defined as an ion heavier than carbon with energy $E>1 \mathrm{MeV} / \mathrm{u}[1,2]$. Such fast ions may appear in nature as fragments of fission processes, in cosmic rays, and also artificially produced at accelerator facilities. Electronic energy losses by such ions constitute over $95 \%$ of the energy deposition [1,2], whereas energy transfer to target atoms is almost negligible along the entire SHI path except near the end of the range. Depending on the target, the ion energy and mass, the electronic stopping power may reach $5-50 \mathrm{keV} /$ nm along the ion trajectory.

Traversing through matter, an SHI initially excites electrons within a few angstroms around its trajectory penetrating to depths of up to hundreds of microns. In its trail the ion may leave regions with structure and phase transformations which constitute a latent ion track: structurally modified material within a diameter of $\sim 1-10 \mathrm{~nm}$ and a length up to hundreds of microns.

Effects of SHI impacts have found a broad range of applications such
as production of track-etched membranes [3], creation of nanodots [4], design of biological materials [5], hadron therapy [6], etc. Irradiation with heavy particles presents certain risks for satellites, spacecraft, and humans in space [7]. Analysis of tracks accumulated in meteorites, in particular, consisting of olivine ( $\mathrm{Mg}_{2} \mathrm{SiO}_{4}$ ), potentially allows to identify a presence of superheavy elements in galactic cosmic rays [8].

Design of experiments, as well as development and implementation of technologies based on SHI irradiation, is hampered by a lack of fundamental understanding of the processes of SHI interaction with solids. Development of a reliable quantitative model of the track formation kinetics would considerably facilitate accumulation of such knowledge.

An SHI impact on a target induces a sequence of processes starting from the energy deposition into the electronic system, to the final relaxation of the excited matter. A swift heavy ion passes by an atom of the target within some attosecond $\left(\sim 10^{-18} \mathrm{~s}\right)$, exciting a number of electrons. At such times, the atoms and electrons of a material can be considered frozen in space. The energy transferred to electrons of a

[^0]target around the SHI trajectory relaxes via various channels. Delta electrons fly outwards from the SHI trajectory, creating cascades of secondary electrons and transferring energy to the lattice (typically on the sub-picosecond timescales, $10^{-15} \mathrm{~s}$ to $10^{-12} \mathrm{~s}$ ) [9]. Simultaneously, deep shell holes, created by an SHI impact, decay via Auger or radiative mechanisms (also during $\sim 10^{-15} \mathrm{~s}$ ) [10]. Atoms react to the transferred energy from the excited electrons and valence holes (during $10^{-13} \mathrm{~s}$ to $10^{-12} \mathrm{~s}$ ) [11], with possible ensuing atomic disorder [12]. Partial or full recovery of the transiently damaged area around an SHI trajectory typically takes $\sim 100 \mathrm{ps}$ [12]. All of these points indicate not only extreme initial excitation of a target but also ultrashort spatial and temporal scales of the track formation kinetics.

Over the past decades, a number of models describing SHI irradiation effects have been developed [13]. They may be categorized into analytical or semiempirical models, and numerical ones. Among the most popular semiempirical models are the two-temperature model (TTM [14], within the SHI community also known as the inelastic thermal spike model [15]), and the Coulomb explosion [16].

The TTM considers only the exchange of the kinetic energy between electrons and atoms of the target, modeled within the thermodynamic equilibrium approximation. The model relies on the coupled thermodiffusion equations for the electronic and the phononic systems. Fitting parameters are introduced empirically to reproduce the experimental results on track diameters [17]. The need to use fitting parameters arises due to inapplicability of the thermodynamic concepts, phononic and diffusion approximations, for description of the nanometric track formation problem at the femtosecond time scale, as will be discussed in this review.

The Coulomb explosion model is based on the assumption that the created charge non-neutrality drives the atomic repulsion thereby creating damaged regions along an SHI path. It assumes that the charge non-neutrality lasts sufficiently long, and the created holes are bound to their parenting ions. These approximations, valid for small molecules and nanoclusters [18,19], are not justified for solids [11]. The Coulomb explosion model is rarely used nowadays for description of SHI interaction with bulk solids.

Numerical models used to describe a fast ion interaction with matter present an important alternative. Powerful and precise ab-initio approaches (such as e.g. time-dependent density functional theory) are time consuming and at present limited mostly to small systems or low particle energies [20,21].

Electronic cascades can be described very efficiently with classical Monte Carlo (MC) simulations [22,23]. Such methods allow simulating the initial stages of electron kinetics induced by an SHI impact [9,24-26]. However, they do not describe the reaction of the atomic lattice to such an energy deposition.

Atomic dynamics can be modeled by means of classical Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations [27-29]. Tracing each atom's trajectory, MD methods provide complete information on the transient state of the atomic system, however, they do not have an access to the state of the electronic system and thus rely on an external input, which is often introduced ad hoc [27,30,31].

In the recent years, it is becoming a new standard practice to build combined (also known as hybrid or multiscale) approaches [32-34]. Such methods combine two or more approaches describing different stages and effects of SHI track formation (e.g. excitation of electronic and atomic subsystems, nonthermal effects etc.) into one model, which then allows to treat various aspects of the problem at hand with their own efficient methods [35].

Due to a large difference between the characteristic times of the electronic and atomic systems of a solid, one of the most common combined approaches became the combination of the TTM for description of electronic evolution with MD describing the atomic dynamics [36-38]. Such methods, however, suffer from the inherent limitations of the models being combined. For example, nonequilibrium electron kinetics, ballistic transport, and processes involving valence
holes cannot be properly described within the TTM [11] - thus neither can they be within a joint TTM-MD.

To alleviate the above mentioned limitations, we recently developed a combined approach that describes all the successive stages of an SHI track formation [12,39-41]. The ion penetration and ensuing nonequilibrium kinetics of electrons, holes, and photons are traced within the MC code TREKIS [9,42], whereas the atomic dynamics is modeled by means of an MD [39]. The model allowed us to describe effects of different electron energy spectra generated by ions of different energies, and for the first time to describe the track formation along the entire SHI trajectory.

An extensive validation of the model against the dedicated experiments was performed in our collaboration, proving a good applicability of the approach [12,39-41]. The model does not use fitting parameters, thereby allowing us to gain qualitatively new insights into the track production process which will be described in this review.

## 2. Experiment

In a series of dedicated experiments, single crystalline $\alpha-\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}, \mathrm{MgO}$ and $\mathrm{Y}_{3} \mathrm{Al}_{5} \mathrm{O}_{12}$ (YAG) specimens were irradiated with 167 MeV Xe and 710 MeV Bi ions at 300 K . The irradiations were performed at fluences ranging from $10^{10}$ to $10^{13} \mathrm{~cm}^{-2}$ at the cyclotron complex of FLNR JINR (Dubna, Russia) in order to investigate both the individual track regime and track overlapping regime. Average Xe ion flux was $\sim 10^{9} \mathrm{~cm}^{-2} \mathrm{~s}^{-1}$, and Bi ion flux was $\sim 3 \cdot 10^{8} \mathrm{~cm}^{-2} \mathrm{~s}^{-1}$. During irradiation, the samples were mounted on water-cooled copper holders maintained at room temperature. Ion beam homogeneity better than $5 \%$ at the surface of an irradiated specimen has been reached using beam scanning in horizontal and vertical directions. High resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) studies were carried out at the Centre for HRTEM at Nelson Mandela University (Port Elizabeth, South Africa). Plane view transmission electron microscopy (TEM) lamellae were extracted within $1 \mu \mathrm{~m}$ from the irradiated surface using an FEI Helios Nanolab 650 focused ion beam (FIB). 30 keV Ga was used for milling and prethinning of samples, while 5 keV Ga was used for final thinning and 1 keV Ga ions were used for polishing. Samples were imaged using a JEOL ARM-200F TEM operating at 200 kV , achieving higher resolution than in the previous TEM analysis [39]. Electron exit wave reconstruction was performed through focal series HRTEM micrographs using the focal and tilt series reconstruction (FTSR) plugin for Digital Micrograph ${ }^{\text {TM }}$ by HREM Research Inc. [43]. Local strain maps were computed from the phase component of restored complex electron exit waves using the geometric phase analysis (GPA) plugin for Digital Micrograph from HREM Research Inc.

## 3. Model

To study the entire sequence of processes occurring during an SHI track formation, we applied a hybrid simulation technique describing the coupled kinetics of the excited electronic and the atomic systems of a material irradiated with high-energy heavy ions. First, the asymptotic trajectory MC code TREKIS [9,42] is used to determine the initial parameters characterizing an excited state of electrons and holes as well as energy transferred to lattice atoms. The calculated radial distribution of the energy transferred into the lattice is then used as input data for the classical molecular dynamics code LAMMPS [44] simulating subsequent lattice relaxation and structure transformations. Below we give a brief description of the both approaches and their combination.

### 3.1. Monte-Carlo model of electronic system excitation

The Monte-Carlo code TREKIS (Time-Resolved Electron Kinetics in SHI Irradiated Solids [9,42]) is an event-by-event (or analog) individual particle simulation model [45-47]. The used scattering cross sections, which are the most essential component of an MC scheme, are based on
the formalism of the dynamic structure factor proposed by L. van Hove [48]. Within the first Born approximation, this approach links the loss function (the inverse imaginary part of the complex dielectric function, CDF) with the cross section of scattering of an incident particle on a spatially and dynamically coupled system of scattering centers. The CDF formalism allows to take into account collective responses of the electronic system and the lattice of a solid to perturbations caused by an SHI and fast electrons generated in an ion track. This method requires only the optical properties of the target material as an input [49], and allows construction of charged particle scattering cross sections. Obtained cross sections are material specific, and do not rely on atomic approximation and the Bragg additivity rule for compounds. In Ref.[9] we reconstructed the CDF from optical data provided by photo-absorption experiments and calculations (refractive index $n$ and extinction coefficient $k$, or the photon attenuation length) for a number of solid materials. The procedure of reconstruction of the CDF is described in detail in Refs. [9,42,49].

Based on these cross sections, TREKIS models the following processes: (a) penetration of a swift heavy projectile resulting in ionization of a target and appearance of primary electrons ( $\delta$-electrons) and holes; (b) scattering of $\delta$-electrons on lattice atoms and target electrons as well as the kinetics of all secondary generations of electrons; (c) Auger decays of core holes, also resulting in production of secondary electrons; (d) radiative decays of core holes, following photon transport and photoabsorption exciting new electrons and holes; (e) valence holes transport and their interaction with target atoms. All details of the numerics used and corresponding cross sections for different processes can be found in Refs. [9,42].

The entire MC procedure is iterated $\sim 10^{3}$ times to gather reliable statistics. As a result, we obtain the temporal dependencies of the radial distributions of the density and energy of electrons as well as holes in the valence band and different atomic shells, and the energy transferred into the atomic system of a target.

### 3.2. Energy transfer to the lattice in SHI tracks

Energy transfer from the excited electronic system to the atomic one is a complex process, involving thermal and nonthermal effects [11]. Thermal effects are the result of the electron-ion energy exchange, while nonthermal ones are an outcome of the potential energy surface modification by significant electron excitation in the close vicinity of the ion trajectory [11].

Both types of the effects are difficult to model comprehensively at femtosecond and nanometric scales of excitation around an SHI path. This motivated us to develop a simplified model that proved to be sufficiently simple and accurate [12,39-41]:
(1) Within 100 fs after the ion passage, the exchange of the kinetic energy, responsible for the thermal effects occurs due to elastic (the total kinetic energy of interacting electrons, holes and atoms is conserved) scattering of generated electrons and holes on atoms. This transfer is traced directly within the MC code in the framework of the CDF formalism [48].
(2) To account for possible transformations of induced potential energy, we assume instant transfer of a large part of the excess electronic energy accumulated in the potential energy of valence holes at 100 fs .

We used the approximation of an instant deposition of the excess energy into a lattice at 100 fs after an ion impact. This sudden increase of the kinetic energies of lattice atoms in the simulation cell assumes assigning random directions to additional momenta provided to atoms in such a transfer. Such approximation enables us, e.g., to describe successfully the radial sizes and structure of SHI tracks in alumina [50] and other materials.

### 3.3. Molecular dynamics simulations of lattice relaxation

Taking into account the initial energy deposited into the lattice, we calculate the velocities of atoms in cylindrical layers assuming Gaussian-like dispersion of the kinetic energy and uniform distribution of momenta of atoms within each layer. This distribution of the velocities is then used to simulate the lattice response applying the classical MD code LAMMPS [44].

For the materials considered here, we used the interatomic forces in YAG in the form of the three-body interaction potential developed in Ref.[51], whereas the interaction between atoms in $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}, ~ \mathrm{MgO}$ and $\mathrm{Mg}_{2} \mathrm{SiO}_{4}$ is described with pair Buckingham-type potentials with parameterization taken from Ref. [52]. The supercells used in the MD simulations have the periodic boundary conditions in all directions. Supercell sizes in directions normal to ion trajectory are $15-25 \mathrm{~nm}$, while the size along an ion path is usually $10-20 \mathrm{~nm}$ (500000-1500000 atoms). For simulations with a track overlap, the cell size was increased up to 35 nm in one direction to accommodate both tracks. An SHI penetration is assumed to be along Z-axis, and the supercell borders in the X - and Y -directions are cooled by the Berendsen thermostat [53] to 300 K with a characteristic time of 0.1 ps . Track evolution is traced up to $\sim 150 \mathrm{ps}$, after which the cell temperature dropped below 350 K , so no structural changes are expected after this time. Visualization of the states of MD cells is performed using OVITO open-source software [54]. TEM images were simulated from MD supercells with the help of muSTEM code [55] in order to compare it with experimental TEM results wherever available.

## 4. Results and discussion

### 4.1. Transient electronic exitations

A penetrating SHI initially deposits its energy into the electronic system within a few angstroms around its trajectory. An example of such an energy deposition, resulting electronic excitation, and their evolution is shown in Fig. 1 for the case of 167 MeV Xe impact in $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$. Both graphs demonstrate propagation of several different modes of excitation from the track center.

The very front of the excitation is produced by the photons that create secondary electrons upon absorption. These photons are produced on sub-fs timescales by radiative decay of core holes created by the SHI [42].

Behind them, two fronts of the electronic ballistic transport occur. The average velocities of motion of the first and second fronts are $\sim 400 \AA / \mathrm{fs}$ and $\sim 20 \AA / \mathrm{fs}$ respectively. The first front is formed by the propagation of the fastest delta-electrons generated in SHI collisions. The second front is a result of a generation of a large amount of plasmons formed due to excitation of the valence band by an SHI. In our MC model, we assume that each produced plasmon decays instantly into a single electron-hole pair, resulting in creation of many electrons with nearly the same energies. The following coherent motion of these electrons outwards from the track center forms the second front in spatial propagation of the excess electron energy. It smoothens out by about 10 to 100 fs similarly to that demonstrated in [47,56]. Including a finite life-time of plasmons could smoothen the second front slightly faster, by a plasmon life-time (typically, sub-femtosecond to a femtosecond).

At times of $\sim 1 \mathrm{fs}$ after the ion passage, the distribution of electrons forms a plateau in the closest proximity of the SHI trajectory. Accumulating a large part of the energy of electrons, this plateau is smoothly decreasing with time. Those slow electrons exhibit a diffusive transport mode. Note that only this fraction of electrons can, in principle, be described within a thermodynamic approach, but not the ballistic fractions described above.

### 4.2. Track structure and formation kinetics

The atomic lattice response to such an energy deposition described above is an extremely fast disorder within the radius of $5-10 \mathrm{~nm}$ by $\sim 1 \mathrm{ps}$. Fig. 6 illustrates the relaxation kinetics of the damaged lattice of $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ supercell in the vicinity of the ion trajectory at times of $1,10,15,20 \mathrm{ps}$ after a passage of 167 MeV Xe . The strongly disordered region of $\sim 5 \mathrm{~nm}$ in diameter can be clearly seen at the beginning of lattice relaxation. This area is surrounded by $\sim 2 \mathrm{~nm}$ cylindrical layer with a distorted crystalline structure. At later times ( $10-20 \mathrm{ps}$ ), the damaged area shrinks due to epitaxial recrystallization of cylindrical layers, resulting in formation of a stable damaged ion track of only 1.8 nm diameter at 100 ps (Fig. 7a).

### 4.3. Recrystallization in SHI tracks

In the previous section, the importance of recrystallization processes for track formation in non-amorphizable dielectric (alumina) was demonstrated. In order to study this process dependence on material properties in detail, we have chosen three oxide materials ( $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}, \mathrm{MgO}$ and $\mathrm{Y}_{3} \mathrm{Al}_{5} \mathrm{O}_{12}$ ) with different lattice structures and comparable energy deposition to the lattice for the same SHI parameters [12].

The calculated energy loss of a 167 MeV Xe ion in $\mathrm{MgO}, \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ and YAG are $21,24.9$ and $25 \mathrm{keV} / \mathrm{nm}$ respectively. Fig. 8 shows the radial and temporal dependencies of the energy of electrons and their density after 167 MeV Xe impact. One can see that the kinetics of the electron system differ only slightly among $\mathrm{MgO}, \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ and YAG, apart from
electron energies at small radii ( $<1 \mathrm{~nm}$ ).
After the electron kinetics, the radial distributions of the energy transferred into the lattices of $\mathrm{MgO}, \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ and YAG are presented in Fig. 9. All these three targets demonstrate similar behavior of electronic excitation and relaxation: formation of two fronts of excess energy propagation and comparable energy deposition to the lattice (a slightly higher energy density in YAG within $\sim 1 \mathrm{~nm}$ around the ion path introduces only a negligible difference, since the volume of this region is very small). Nearly identical kinetics of excitation allows us to use these three systems to isolate an effect of lattice relaxation on the track formation in oxides. The study of lattice relaxation processes can help to understand the influence of a structure and a force field of atoms, as will be discussed below.

Despite close energy deposition and similar initial damage at $\sim 1 \mathrm{ps}$ after the impacts [12,39], Fig. 10 shows that the SHI irradiation of these targets leads to formation of quite different damaged structures.

MgO has only a few point defects in the proximity of the ion trajectory with no clear SHI track (a similar situation occurs for 700 MeV Bi irradiation). The absence of significant SHI damage in MgO was also detected in experiments [62], and due to the absence of discernible tracks no TEM imaged track is shown for this material in Fig. 10. In order to directly compare the results of modeling with experimental TEM data, we simulated TEM images from the calculated MD atomic snapshots with help of MuSTEM code [55] (these insets are labeled as SimTEM). The simulated HRTEM image confirms that the low damage density is not visible in TEM.

The simulation predicted that 167 MeV Xe in $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ forms a discontinuous crystalline track of $\sim 2 \pm 0.3 \mathrm{~nm}$ in diameter, which was confirmed by high resolution TEM images and was discussed in detail in Ref. [39]. The 100 ps image with experimental and simulated HRTEM insets is the same as in Fig. 7a. Typical FIB lamellae have a thickness around $30-40 \mathrm{~nm}$ and thus such small localized areas of distorted crystal are always imaged within a relatively thick region of unaffected material with a near perfect crystal structure. HRSTEM resolution is maximized in the near surface region of the foil. This limits atomic resolution to the top few nm of the specimen and therefore this technique is not optimal for imaging discontinuous tracks where the near surface region of the foil might be relatively undisturbed. However, HRSTEM is well suited to imaging continuous, cylindrical amorphous tracks such as those in YAG.

A calculated track in YAG has a cylindrical shape with the size of $\sim 5.1 \pm 0.2 \mathrm{~nm}$ and a completely amorphous structure, in a reasonable agreement with the experiment, Fig. 10. The experiment confirms that the tracks in YAG are amorphous with a comparable diameter of $\sim 6.5 \pm 0.6 \mathrm{~nm}$ based on 97 measured tracks. The inset shows an annular bright field HRSTEM micrograph of a typical track in YAG. In this imaging mode, the central region with a relatively flat contrast represents an amorphous track core. The amorphous-crystalline boundary can be seen just at the edge of the central circle where atomic columns become visible. SimTEM insets in Fig. 10 show similar damage structures as experimental TEM data: distorted crystalline tracks in $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ and amorphous tracks in YAG. Measurement of the YAG track diameter from the simulated ABF image yielded a diameter of 3.5 nm which is smaller than the calculated value of 5.1 nm . The difference is once again due to the inability of the TEM to detect extremely small distortions in low concentration through the projected foil. The size discrepancy between the MD predicted track diameter and experimental diameter is most probably related to the fact that the employed potential predicts a melting temperature of 3300 K for YAG while the experimentally determined temperature is around 2213 K [12].

In order to understand the nature of the recrystallization effect in SHI tracks we checked whether there is any ordering effect of bonds between atoms at the initial stage of track formation, which can enhance or suppress recrystallization. The analysis of pair distribution functions of studied materials in Ref.[12] did not reveal any considerable difference between $\mathrm{MgO}, \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ and YAG at the initial moments of relaxation of excited tracks (at $\sim 1 \mathrm{ps}$ after an ion impact). Thus, we concluded that all materials transiently lose their structure, and recrystallization starts from a similar state with an initial short order present in these oxides.

One can also note that the recrystallization efficiency correlates with "simplicity" (or "complexity") of the target lattice structure [12]. Indeed, complete and fast track recrystallization is observed in MgO having a "simple" lattice structure with the smallest number of the peaks in the pair correlation function. A similar picture was observed in

MD modeling of LiF and NaCl exhibiting no continuous tracks [63]. However, large amorphous tracks are detected in YAG with the most "complex" lattice structure (the largest number of peaks). Ref. [41] showed that SHI irradiation of olivine ( $\mathrm{Mg}_{2} \mathrm{SiO}_{4}$, studied in more detail in the next section) with a "complex" structure produces amorphous tracks which conforms to the presented trend. The lattice of $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ fills an intermediate "complexity" here, showing partial track formation. The observed correlation allows us to conclude that the "complexity" of the structure can be used for a crude estimate of the expected recrystallization efficiency in SHI tracks. However, the complexity of a lattice structure cannot be treated as the sole factor governing damage formation in SHI tracks, but should be complemented with more general considerations, involving dynamical properties of materials [12].

In order to study the dynamics of the recrystallization in more detail, we have considered the relaxation of sublattices, which revealed that oxygen atoms settle in their sites at epitaxially restoring interface of the crystalline matrix faster than Mg (or Al ) atoms in MgO (or $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ ) [12]. Metallic atoms then adjust to a layer of oxygen that has already been built. Fig. 11 shows temporal evolution of the sublattices of the studied targets at different times after the ion passage. A diameter of the damaged region in the oxygen sublattice is smaller than in $\mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{Al})$ sublattices in the case of $\mathrm{MgO}\left(\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}\right)$ during the relaxation stage.

The atomic mobility is important for recrystallization. We simulated the diffusion coefficients of atoms in studied oxides [12] to check their importance in the track formation kinetics. Since we are interested in the behavior of atoms near the interface between the transiently damaged (melted) region and the surrounding crystalline matrix, and the migration velocities of atoms were studied at the melting temperature as well as at temperatures $\pm 100 \mathrm{~K}$ from the melting point. The melting temperatures were calculated for each particular interatomic potential used for the studied materials. The diffusion coefficients were determined as the slopes of mean-squared displacements versus time.

Atoms in MgO demonstrate the highest diffusion coefficients ( $\sim 12 \mathrm{nm}^{2} \mathrm{~ns}^{-1}$ ) in the molten regime, while the diffusion coefficient of oxygen in $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}\left(\sim 2.5 \mathrm{~nm}^{2} \mathrm{~ns}^{-1}\right)$ does not differ from that in YAG. It should be mentioned that other theoretical works report diffusion coefficients of $19 \mathrm{~nm}^{2} / \mathrm{ns}$ for MgO [64] and $\sim 1.5 \mathrm{~nm}^{2} / \mathrm{ns}$ for $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ [65]. Below the melting points, all three targets show almost zero diffusivity. Interestingly, $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{Zr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{7}$ has a higher migration velocity at submelting temperatures than $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{Ti}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{7}$ and this difference strongly increases with decrease of temperature, which is consistent with the results presented in [66], where strong recrystallization rate of $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{Zr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{7}$ was shown, whereas $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{Ti}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{7}$ does not demonstrate damage recovery within an ion track.

### 4.4. Damage along SHI trajectory

Macroscopic approaches used in the community have problems with accurate prediction of variations of the SHI tracks size with the ion penetration depth [67,68]. This occurs due to their inapplicability at the femtosecond temporal and nanometric spatial scales of material excitation around trajectories of swift heavy ions [11]. As a result, these models use fitting parameters a-posteriori reproducing results of experiments on SHI induced structure changes at fixed ion velocities (e.g. by using TEM images or indirect measurements of accumulated damage).

Our developed approach [39,40] enabled us to avoid unnecessary assumptions and fitting in simulations of structure transformations in tracks. This allowed us to describe lattice excitation and structure modifications at different points along the penetration depths covering almost the entire ion range. In the recent work [41], the model was applied to study a coupled effect of the swift heavy ion velocity and energy loss on formation of a nanometric damaged region around the entire trajectories of nonrelativistic $\mathrm{Xe}(30-1650 \mathrm{MeV})$ and U $(45-15000 \mathrm{MeV})$ ions in olivine ( $\mathrm{Mg}_{2} \mathrm{SiO}_{4}$, in forsterite state). We also calculated track sizes and ion stopping for hypothetical nuclei ${ }_{120}^{304} \mathrm{Ubn}$ to cover a wider range of atomic numbers and demonstrate predictive capabilities of our model. Consideration of such superheavy elements is important for their search in galactic cosmic rays with the help of etching of solid natural detectors from meteorites, which dictated our choice of the material for this study [69]. From the fundamental point of view, the choice of the material here is irrelevant, since the conclusions drawn can qualitatively be transferred to other amorphizable materials as well.

Fig. 12 presents the energy losses of $\mathrm{Xe}, \mathrm{U}$ and Ubn ions in forsterite calculated with TREKIS code. The modeled ions energy samples are shown as dots in this figure. The results of the SRIM [70] code for Xe and U demonstrated for comparison show an overall good agreement with the presented data which confirms applicability of the model to $\mathrm{Mg}_{2} \mathrm{SiO}_{4}$. Unfortunately, SRIM code does not provide the energy losses for Ubn ions.

Fig. 15 shows the dependence of the track size on the U ion range and energy. Calculated points are accompanied with the electronic energy loss curves. The peak energy loss value (Bragg peak) does not coincide with the maximal structural damage region: the maximal ion energy loss does not produce maximal damage in a material. The maximal damage along the track range lies systematically deeper along the ion trajectory than the Bragg peak position [41].

The discrepancy between the maximal structure damage position and the energy loss maximum can be considered in terms of the velocity effect and the efficiency of energy transfer to the lattice. The track size dependence on the ion energy (or velocity) is ultimately defined by a
spectrum of excited electrons. The electron kinetics producing the atomic heating mainly manifests itself as interplay of two factors:

1) For slow ions, a track size increases with increase of ion energy loss, which is a consequence of a larger amount of the total energy deposited to the electronic subsystem (and a larger number of electrons generated by an SHI).
2) Track size depends strongly on the efficiency of energy transfer to the lattice, which, in turn, depends on the ion velocity, which determines the spectrum of generated electrons. This spectrum defines the following kinetics of the electronic subsystem and energy transfer to the lattice, finally producing a specific profile of the lattice energy for each ion speed.

In Ref. [41] it was demonstrated that the maximum of efficiency is at the lower ion energies than the Bragg-peak. A maximum of the product of these two factors is situated at intermediate energies between these two maxima, and is proportional to the track size. Thus, the track size maxima appear systematically at lower energies of ions (correspondingly, deeper along an SHI path) than the Bragg-peak.

### 4.5. Overlap of SHI tracks

The recrystallization of SHI tracks is very fast ( $\sim 20-50 \mathrm{ps}$ ), which makes it impossible to observe it directly using modern experimental techniques. On the other hand, an initially molten zone produced by an ion should strongly affect the existing defects, for example, tracks created by earlier passed ions. So, one of the possible ways of an indirect study of processes of damage recovery within a track is an investigation of influence of hot tracks on existing ones. This, in turn, can be done experimentally studying structures of overlapping ion tracks at high fluences $\left(\sim 10^{12} \mathrm{~cm}^{-2}\right)$. To study these processes, the structures produced by close impacts of ions are compared for amorphizable (YAG) and non-amorphizable ( $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ ) solids.

In contrast to $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$, the influence radius of the second track in YAG
is rather small. Fig. 18a and b show the results of simulation of sequential 700 MeV Bi ion impacts in YAG at distances of 9 and 10 nm between the ion trajectories. For a close impact of Bi ions in YAG (Fig. 18c) we have only an experimental image for 1 GeV Bi ion irradiation. The radius of track interaction in YAG is small, which may be considered as an experimental evidence of absence or very low recrystallization efficiency of YAG in tracks of high-energy heavy ions. In contrast, this influence distance in alumina is much larger which is an evidence of the efficient recrystallization in an SHI track. Therefore, studying of track structures in overlapping regime allows estimating the size of the transiently molten zone.

## 5. Conclusion

We reviewed here results of our studies of stages of the kinetics of swift heavy ion tracks formation in insulators. The developed hybrid approach combining Monte Carlo and Molecular Dynamics describes in detail each successive stage, starting from the SHI induced electronic excitation, transport of electrons, valence holes and photons, energy exchange with the target lattice, transient excitation and relaxation of the atomic system, and ultimate damage formation. The used efficient way of coupling the MC and MD schemes proved to be sufficiently simple yet produce accurate results, validated by high-resolution transmission electron microscopy analysis of SHI irradiated samples. The model is free of any fitting parameters and thus has a full predictive power.

The developed model enabled us to identify the valence holes as the main contributor into the heating of the atomic lattice in insulators. Transfer of the kinetic energy of valence holes delivers the major part of energy in the track center (within around 1 nm ), while the potential energy of the electron-hole pairs produces longer tails of the energy distribution (from $\sim 1$ to about 5 nm ).

The ballistic transport of electrons is the main driving force beyond the velocity effect. The electronic spectrum defines how the energy is distributed in space and time prior to and during the heating of atomic system. In particular, the electronic transport and the energy redistribution result into a systematic mismatch between the deposited dose along the SHI path and the produced damage: the maximal track radius does not coincide with the Bragg peak.

The ultrafast atomic heating by holes and electrons results into formation of the transient large disordered area (up to $\sim 5-10 \mathrm{~nm}$ ) in studied materials, which then may recrystallize and shrink to a final observable smaller track. For the same initially disordered area, in nonamorphizable materials the recrystallization recovers the crystalline structure to a nearly perfect state. In amorphizable materials the transient damaged region coincides with the final one with almost no recovery. In studied targets, we identified a correlation between the simplicity of the crystal structure and the efficiency of the material recovery in SHI tracks.

The transient large disorder plays the major role in track annealing at high ion fluences in $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$. Overlap of two successive tracks may
anneal the first one, if the second track is sufficiently close - such that its transient heated area covers the prior track and significantly heats it up. With increase of the distance between the ion impact points, partial overlap of tracks may lead to a connecting damaged structure between the two. An interaction between the tracks seizes when the second track is too far for the transient heating to affect the prior track.
