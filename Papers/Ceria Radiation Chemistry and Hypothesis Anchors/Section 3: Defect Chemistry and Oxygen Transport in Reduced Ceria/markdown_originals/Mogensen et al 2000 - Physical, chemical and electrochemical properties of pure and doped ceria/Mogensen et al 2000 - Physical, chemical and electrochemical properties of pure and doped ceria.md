#### Abstract

This paper gives an extract of available data on the physical, chemical, electrochemical and mechanical properties of pure and doped ceria, predominantly in the temperature range from 200 to $1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Several areas are pointed out where further research is needed in order to make a better basis for the evaluation of the real potential and limits for the practical application of ceria in solid oxide fuel cells and other solid state electrochemical devices. 2000 Elsevier Science B.V. All rights reserved.


Keywords: Ceria; Mixed ionic and electronic conductor; Solid oxide fuel cell; Electrolyte; Anode

## 1. Introduction

Ceria-based materials have been very widely investigated. The number of hits in a literature search on the keyword 'ceria' for the period from June 1993 until the end of 1998 was 560 , and this search was restricted to main journal articles only. A total ceria search in Chemical Abstracts covering this century reveals thousands of references. Therefore, within the available frames, it will be possible to cover only a limited number of properties in any depth. For example, the area of the catalytic properties of ceria will just be mentioned very briefly in the context of electrochemical properties even though this is one of the areas where most papers are being produced.

[^0]Also, several books and numerous review articles on the topic of oxide conductors have been written, e.g., Refs. [1-6], which all cover ceria to some extent. Recently a specific review of ceria-based solid electrolytes was published [7]. However, it is in no way the intention of the present paper to substitute any of these reviews. The purpose is to contribute to the construction of a basis, which will make it possible to draw more reliable conclusions about the potential and limitations of the applications of ceria based materials as electrolyte and electrodes in high temperature electrochemical devices, especially in solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC). In this context it is necessary to look into a number of different properties of ceria such as mechanical properties, chemical stability and electrical conductivity.

## 2. Defect and redox chemistry

### 2.1. Types of defects and reactions

When $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ is reduced to $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ defects are formed in the form of $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$, which in the Kröger-Vink-notation is written as $\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}$ as the $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ has one negative charge compared to the normal lattice In early works, such as those of Kofstad and Hed [11], Kevane [12], Blumenthal et al. [13], and Iwasaka and Katsura [14], it was discussed whether these substitutional negative defects were balanced by some of the $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ going on interstitial sites as $\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{i}}^{\cdot \cdot}$ or by oxide ion vacancies, $\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}^{\cdot \cdot}$. Later works using oxygen self-diffusion studies [15-17]) in nonstoichiometric $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ appear to show that the behaviour was consistent with the oxygen vacancy model. Now it is generally agreed that the main compensating defects in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ are oxygen vacancies. Faber et al.
[18] examined the electron density distribution using x-ray diffraction (XRD), and they concluded that the amount of interstitial Ce is less than about $0.1 \%$ of the total defect concentration in $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.91}$.

The process of ceria reduction may be written as
$\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{O}}+2 \mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}=\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}($ gas $)+\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}^{\cdot \cdot}+2 \mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}$

Oxide vacancies may also be introduced by doping with oxides of metals with lower valencies, e.g., by dissolution of CaO or $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ :
$\mathrm{CaO}=\mathrm{Ca}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime \prime}+\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}^{\prime \prime}+\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{O}}$
$\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}=2 \mathrm{Gd}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}+\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}^{\bullet \bullet}+3 \mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{O}}$

And oxide vacancies may be removed by doping with oxides of higher valencies:
$\mathrm{Nb}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}+\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}^{\bullet \bullet}=2 \mathrm{Nb}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\cdot}+4 \mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{O}}$

Reaction (2.1) may also take place in doped ceria, and doping will naturally affect the equilibrium of Reaction (2.1) by changing the concentration of oxide vacancies and by decreasing the concentration of $\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}$.

Assuming no interaction between the various defects (only valid for very low concentrations) the law of mass action is valid. Applied on Reaction (2.1) this gives:
$\left[\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}^{\cdot \cdot}\right]\left[\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}\right]^{2} P \mathrm{O}_{2}^{1 / 2}=$ constant
as the concentrations of $\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{O}}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}$ are about constant. In the case of undoped ceria [ $\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}^{\cdot \cdot}$ ] $=x$ and $\left[\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}\right]=2 x$. Insertion in Reaction (2.5) and rearrangement gives:
$x=$ constant $P \mathrm{O}_{2}^{-1 / 6}$

For doped ceria the concentration of vacancies may also be regarded as constant, and thus the result is
$x=$ constant $P \mathrm{O}_{2}^{-1 / 4}$
where $x$ is defined by $2 x=\left[\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}\right]$.

### 2.2. Thermodynamic properties

Extensive studies of the thermodynamics of Reaction (2.1) have been carried out on pure ceria in
which $x$ was measured as a function of oxygen partial pressure. The methods used were thermogravimetry, coulometric titration and electrical conductivity (the electronic conductivity is proportional to $x$ for $x<10^{-2}$, see below). It was possible to divide the $P \mathrm{O}_{2}$-range into intervals where $x$ is proportional to $\mathrm{PO}_{2}^{-1 / n}$. Near stoichiometry ( $x<$ 0.006 ) an $n$-value of 5 has in many cases been found. In the composition range $0.006<x<0.1$ it is reported that $2<n<5$. The region $0.1<x<0.3$ must be divided into smaller intervals with $n$ increasing rapidly with $x$, up to $n$-values of approximately 30 (see e.g., Refs. [19-25]). In Fig. 1, which gives $-\Delta G_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}$ at $1100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ versus $-\log x, n$-values are shown for the different $P \mathrm{O}_{2}$-intervals. It also shows that the regions with different $n$-values have different but linear behaviour in this plot.

The simple theory of non-interacting-defects predicts an $n$-value of 6 , which should at least be obtained for low values of $x$, and indeed this value has now been reported [26,27]. The difficulty in obtaining the $n$-value of 6 was explained by Dawicke and Blumenthal [28]. They determined that for values of $x$ less than $10^{-2.4}$, the observed $\mathrm{PO}_{2}^{-1 / 5}$ dependency of both the conductivity and the deviation from stoichiometry, was due to the existence of vacancies caused by Ca impurities. The oxygen partial pressure dependence of $-\frac{1}{6}$ is only valid for pure $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$. In order to obtain the oxygen partial pressure dependence of the conductivity and $x$ of $-\frac{1}{6}$, Chang and Blumenthal [29] doped pure $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ ( 300 ppm CaO ) with $\mathrm{Ta}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}$ in order to remove the oxide vacancies (see Reaction (2.4)), and the authors actually showed that the conductivity was proportional to $\mathrm{PO}_{2}^{-1 / 6}$ as predicted.

The strong increase in $n$-values with increasing $x$ above $x=0.1$ is not explainable in terms of noninteracting defects and the law of mass action. This may be explained by a series of discrete ordering phases separated by two-phase regions (also see Section 3.1 and Fig. 16 [below]). A similar but much weaker type of development of $n$-values versus $x$ was reported for $10 \mathrm{~mol} \%$ gadolinia, samaria and calcia doped ceria at $900^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ by Schneider et al. [30], even though they did not interpret the increasing $n$-values directly in terms of formation of ordering phases.

Riess et al. [31] measured the chemical potential
of oxygen in nonstoichiometric $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ as a function of $x\left(10^{-4} \leq x \leq 10^{-2}\right)$ and temperature (873-1073 K ), by coulometric titration using an electrochemical cell based on yttria-stabilised zirconia (YSZ) (determined by measuring the EMF (ElectroMotive Force) of the cell, using air as a reference). The results of their work and other previous work [23,32,33] are summarised in Fig. 2, which shows $x$ (in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ ) as a function of the oxygen equilibrium pressure; a fair agreement is observed.

Garnier et al. [34] and Park et al. [35] investigated ceria doped with $10 \mathrm{~mol} \% \mathrm{CaO}$. Zachau-Christiansen et al. [36,37] measured the relationship between stoichiometry and $\mathrm{PO}_{2}$ for pure ceria and $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ doped $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\left(\mathrm{Ce}_{1-y} \mathrm{Gd}_{y} \mathrm{O}_{2-y / 2}, y=0.05\right.$, 0.18 and 0.4) at $1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Schneider et al. [30] investigated $\mathrm{Sm}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}, \mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ and CaO doped $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}, y=0.2,0.2$ and 0.1 , respectively (i.e. the same nominal vacancy concentration in all three cases). The temperature range was from 700 to $900^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Wang et al. [38,39] studied the reduction of $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ doped $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ with $y=0.1$ and 0.2 over a temperature range from 700 to $1100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Results are shown in Figs. 3-5. Note the changes in slope of the pure ceria curve in Fig. 3 at around $P \mathrm{O}_{2}=10^{-15} \mathrm{~atm}$. This is believed to be due to the strong interaction between defects resulting in ordered phases. (Below $648^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ a phase transition takes place, see Section 3). This is not seen in the curves of the doped cerias where no phase change takes place (see Section 3). However, interactions are expected to occur, and we see indications of this in the work of Schneider et al. [30] in the variation of the $n$-values with $x$, i.e., $n$ decreases with increasing $x$ for small $x$ and then increases again. This type of behaviour is difficult to explain by any type of defects following the law of mass action, c.f. Toft Sørensen's work, Fig. 1.

The partial free energy of formation (per $\mathrm{mol} \mathrm{O}_{2}$ ) of a given nonstoichiometric ceria, $\Delta G_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}$, is given by
$\Delta \bar{G}_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}=R T \ln P \mathrm{O}_{2}$
where $\mathrm{PO}_{2}$ is the oxygen partial pressure which is in equilibrium with this ceria. (Note that Reaction (2.1) only operates with $\frac{1}{2}$ a $\mathrm{mol} \mathrm{O}_{2}$ ). Combining this with $\Delta G_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}=\Delta H_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}-T \Delta S_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}$ and rearranging it gives
$\ln P \mathrm{O}_{2}=\left(\Delta \bar{H}_{\mathrm{O}_{2}} / R\right)(1 / T)-\Delta \bar{S}_{\mathrm{O}_{2}} / R$

## 3. Structure and phase equilibria

### 3.1. Pure ceria

Even though the ceria fluorite phase is very tolerant to reduction and dissolution of other oxides, there are naturally some limits. These are described below together with the changes in the unit cell size, which occur with reduction and/or dissolution of other oxides.

### 3.1.1. Phase diagram of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$

During the early 1950s Brauer and Gradinger [43] and Bevan [44] studied the structure of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ using powder XRD. They revealed the existence of the phases $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.812}$, $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.782}$, and $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.719}$, which were thought to be formed as a result of ordering in the cation and anion sublattices. Based on the work of Brauer and Holtschmidt [45,46], Brauer et al. [47], Brauer and Gingerich [48] and Bevan and Kordis [32] a phase diagram for the system $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ to $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.8}$ was constructed. It showed that at room temperature a miscibility gap exists between $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.81}$, which contracts with increasing temperature and closes at $685^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ at a composition of $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.92} \cdot$

In the lower temperature region, the phase $\mathrm{Ce}_{9} \mathrm{O}_{16}$ ( $=\mathrm{CeO}_{1.77}$ ) was reported by Bevan [44] to have a rhombohedral symmetry. However, Anderson and Wuensch [49] concluded that there was no intermediate phase corresponding to this composition, but rather a mixture of phases with compositions $\mathrm{Ce}_{11} \mathrm{O}_{20}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{7} \mathrm{O}_{12}$. Bevan [44] had also reported that the $\delta$-phase, $\mathrm{Ce}_{11} \mathrm{O}_{20}$, shows rhombohedral symmetry and exists over a broad composition range of $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.804}$ to $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.812}$. Anderson and Wuensch reported the phase to be bcc, with the space group Ia 3 . The cell was related to that of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ and had twice the cell edge of the fluorite structure. Thus, up to 1975, there appeared to be confusion over the intermediate phases in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$.

Ray et al. [50], therefore, examined the system $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ using XRD and neutron diffraction to de-
termine if there was a series of homologues of $\mathrm{Ce}_{n} \mathrm{O}_{2 n-2}$ ( $n=$ an integer). Four distinct phases, corresponding to $n=7,9,10$ and 11 were found. Aside from the phase $\mathrm{Ce}_{7} \mathrm{O}_{12}\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{1.714}\right)$ which showed rhombohedral symmetry based on $<111>$ vacancy strings, structures of the other three phases were not determined. However, the authors did suggest that they were of lower symmetry such as monoclinic or triclinic.

More recently, Ricken et al. [51], Riess et al. [52] and Körner et al. [53] investigated the phase transformations in reduced ceria using specific heat, EMF, dilatometry and XRD measurements. Fig. 14 shows the phase diagram of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ determined by specific heat measurements. They concluded that $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ can be reduced to nonstoichiometric compositions, $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$, where $1.7 \leq 2-x \leq 2$. For $T>921 \mathrm{~K} \left(648^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)$ and $2>2-x>1.818$, only the $\alpha$-phase exists. For $T<722 \mathrm{~K}\left(449^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right), \mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ forms a discrete set of compositions. Körner et al. [53] also studied the volume changes associated with the phase transformation across the 722 K -line as a function of the $\mathrm{O} / \mathrm{Ce}$ ratio. The results are shown in Fig. 15. On cooling, the maximum linear dimensional change amounted to about $0.1 \%$. The difference
between heating and cooling is suggested to be due to undercooling during the temperature decrease.

Toft Sørensen [24] studied the relation between $P \mathrm{O}_{2}$ and $x$ (in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ ) by thermogravimetry in the temperature range $900-1400^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, i.e., well within the $\alpha$-phase region. Based on the obtained relations (see also Section 3) he suggested that the $\alpha$-phase transformed into ordered intermediate phases with compositions following the series $\mathrm{Ce}_{n} \mathrm{O}_{2 n-2}$ as shown in Fig. 16. It has been discussed that the nonstoichiometry may be regarded as a simple solid solution of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$. The special feature is that $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$ or $\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce},}^{\prime}$ the small polaron, is mobile in contrast to many other dopant cations. If it can be regarded as a simple solid solution, one consequence is that the lattice parameter should follow Vegard's rule, i.e., that a linear relationship exists between lattice parameter and the concentration of the solute. The slope of this straight line is termed Vegard's slope. Kim [59] published an empirical relation between ionic radius of the metal ion of oxides (dopants) dissolved in ceria (and other fluorite structured oxides)

$$
a=0.5413+\Sigma\left(0.00220 \Delta r_{k}+0.00015 \Delta z_{k}\right) m_{k}
$$

where $a$ (in nm) is the lattice constant of the ceria solid solution at room temperature, $\Delta r_{k}$ (in nm ) is the difference in ionic radius ( $r_{k}-r_{\mathrm{Ce}}$ ) of the $k$ th dopant and the $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}$-radius, which in eightfold coordination is 0.097 nm according to Shannon [60], $\Delta \mathrm{z}_{k}$ is the valence ( $z_{k}-4$ ), and $m_{k}$ is the mole percent of the $k$ th dopant in the form of $\mathrm{MO}_{x}$. It is noted that the Kim's lattice constant of the pure ceria is 0.000166 nm higher than the accepted value of 0.541134 nm [9]. Kim verified his relations against a considerable amount of data, and his analysis of the uncertainty gave a standard error of $3 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~nm}$. If the values for $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.5}\left(r_{\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}}=0.1143 \mathrm{~nm} \Rightarrow \Delta r=0.0173 \mathrm{~nm}\right.$, and $\Delta z=-1$ ) are inserted it gives:

$$
\begin{aligned}
a= & 0.5413 \mathrm{~nm}+m \times 2.306 \\
& \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~nm} \mathrm{~mol} \%^{-1} \mathrm{CeO}_{1.5}
\end{aligned}
$$

i.e., a Vegard's slope of $2.306 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~nm} \mathrm{~mol} \%^{-1} \mathrm{CeO}_{1.5}$. (And as $m($ in $\%)=200 x$ ( $x$ in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ ), we
get for homogeneous samples at room temperature: $a=0.5413 \mathrm{~nm}+x \times 4.612 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~nm}$ ).

Hong and Virkar [61] analysed the relationship between concentration of $\mathrm{LnO}_{1.5}$ in ceria (and zirconia) and lattice parameter of the fluorite lattice ( $\mathrm{Ln}=$ lanthanide). When we reduce their formula we get for the ceria case (using the symbols described above)

$$
\begin{aligned}

\end{aligned}

i.e., a Vegard slope of $2.740 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~nm} \mathrm{~mol} \%^{-1} \mathrm{CeO}_{1.5}$.

Ray et al. [50] plotted the pseudocubic lattice parameter measured at room temperature in the $\alpha+ \delta$-phase composition region in the phase diagram as a function of oxygen to metal ratio in pure ceria. For $\mathrm{O} / \mathrm{M}$ ratios between 2 and 1.8 a straight line was obtained. For $\mathrm{O} / \mathrm{M}$ below 1.8 a deviation from the straight line is observed. From this plot we derive a Vegard slope of $2.25 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~nm} \mathrm{~mol} \%^{-1} \mathrm{CeO}_{1.5}$. This is about $5 \%$ lower than the value obtained from Kim's formula. The observation that Vegard's rule is obeyed even in a two-phase system invalidates this as a criteria from which it may be determined whether $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ can be regarded as a simple solid solution of $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ or not. However, the Vegard's slope concept is very useful as described below.

The detailed reason for the expansion on reduction of ceria is often discussed. The problem may be phrased as: (1) is it because the $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-ion is bigger than the $\mathrm{Ce}^{4+}$-ion? or (2) is it because the oxide vacancy volume is bigger than the oxide ion volume? One reason for the questions is that the effective ion
radius is dependent on how many nearest neighbours the ion has (its coordination number). Hong and Virkar [61] analysed this question, and concluded that the reason is that of point (1). They introduced the concept of an oxide vacancy radius. For ceria the oxide vacancy radius was found to be 0.1164 nm at room temperature which is significantly smaller than the radius of the oxide ion of 0.138 nm .

### 3.2. Doped ceria

The ceria fluorite structure is also very tolerant to dissolution of lower valent metal oxides other than $\mathrm{Ce}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ up to high concentrations, above $40 \%$ in some cases, see, e.g., Etsell and Flengas [2], and Bevan and Summerville [64]. There is, however, a lot of contradicting information in the literature about the solubility limits.

### 3.2.1. Solubility of other oxides and change in lattice parameters

Rare earth oxides and yttria are highly soluble in ceria, about $40 \%$ or more [ 2,64 ]. For temperatures in the range of 1450 to $1650^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ Eguchi et al. [65] report the solubility limits $23 \mathrm{~mol} \%$ for $\mathrm{CaO}, 8 \mathrm{~mol} \%$ for SrO and very small for MgO and BaO . The solubility of CaO in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ was reported by Keler et al. [66] to be $15 \mathrm{~mol} \%$ at $1600^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Zachau-Christiansen et al. [37] reported evidence that the solubility of CaO is a little below $10 \mathrm{~mol} \%$ at $1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ in samples which were kept at temperature for 40 days, and during that period they were reduced several times to an $\mathrm{O} / \mathrm{M}$ ratio of 1.75 . The solubility of MgO is about $2 \%, \mathrm{SrO} 9 \%$ and $\mathrm{Sc}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3} 5 \%$ at 1600,1600 and $1750^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, respectively, according to the compilation by Etsell and Flengas [2].

Kim [59] argues that the solubility limit of a solute depends on the elastic energy, $W$, which is introduced in the lattice due to differences in ionic radius. The larger the elastic energy per substituted ion, the lower is the solubility. The relation between $W$ and the change in the lattice parameter, $\Delta a$, due to formation of a substitutional solid solution is given by:

$$
W=6 G a_{0}(\Delta a)^{2}
$$

where $G$ is the shear modulus of ceria. $\Delta a$ is
governed by Vegard's slope for the given solute. This implies that the highest solubilities should be achieved for a Vegard's slope of $S_{\mathrm{v}}=0$. Based on analogy with $\mathrm{ThO}_{2}$ [59], it seems reasonable to assume that the solubility limit at $1500-1600^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is about $60 \mathrm{~mol} \%$ for rare earth cations with ionic radius near $r_{\mathrm{c}}$ and close to zero if $\left(S_{\mathrm{v}}\right)^{2}$ is above ca. $6 \times 10^{-8}\left(\mathrm{~nm} \mathrm{~mol} \%^{-1}\right)^{2}$.

\end{aligned}
$$

for trivalent dopants, and

$$
\begin{aligned}

\end{aligned}
$$

From Hong and Virkar’s formula (Eq. (3.3)) another type of expression for the trivalent dopants is obtained
$S_{\mathrm{V} 3, H}=0.023027(\mathrm{~mol} \%)^{-1}\left(r_{k}-0.1024 \mathrm{~nm}\right)$

## 4. Conductivity

### 4.1. Electronic conductivity of pure ceria

Forty years ago $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ was already known to be an electrical conductor. Ruloph [72] found that the conductivity was proportional to $\mathrm{PO}_{2}^{1 / 5.7}$ at 1260 K . Noddack and Walch [19] concluded that the ionic transference number for sintered $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ is less than 0.002 in the temperature range of $600-1300^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, i.e., the material is an electronic conductor. It was concluded (e.g. Refs. [20,73]) that $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ between $200^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and $1300^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is an n-type semiconductor. Vinokurov et al. [74] described the electrical conductivity of $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ (using a single crystal) as being mixed n-type and oxide ion conduction. Even though this is correct, the ionic conductivity of pure reduced ceria at $1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is less than $3 \%$ of the total con-
ductivity at a $P \mathrm{O}_{2}$ of $10^{-6} \mathrm{~atm}$ and even lower at lower $\mathrm{PO}_{2}$ (see, e.g., Mogensen et al. [58]). The maximum ionic conductivity at $1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is estimated to be ca. $7 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~S} \mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$ for $\mathrm{CeO}_{1.9}$. The total conductivity for this condition is $2.5 \mathrm{~S} \mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$ ).

Based on works such as those of Blumenthal and Panlener [75], Blumenthal and Hofmaier [21], Tuller and Nowick [76], and Naik and Tien [77], it is now generally agreed that the n-type conductivity takes place by small polaron transport. According to Tuller and Nowick a small polaron is a defect created when an electronic carrier becomes trapped at a given site as a consequence of the displacement of adjacent atoms or ions. The entire defect, i.e., the electron (or electron hole) plus distortion, migrates by a thermally activated hopping.

The small polaron in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$ may thus be identified as $\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}$. The electronic conductivity, $\sigma_{\mathrm{e}}$, may be expressed by
$\sigma_{\mathrm{e}}=\left[\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}\right] e \mu$
where [ $\mathrm{Ce}_{\mathrm{Ce}}^{\prime}$ ] is the concentration of $\mathrm{Ce}^{3+}$-ions $\mathrm{cm}^{-3}, e$ is the charge of the electron (in As) and $\mu$ is the mobility in $\left(\mathrm{cm}^{2} \mathrm{Vs}^{-1}\right)$. The mobility has the form
$\mu=(B / T) \exp \left(-E_{\mathrm{H}} / k T\right)$
where $E_{\mathrm{H}}$ is the activation energy for the small polaron hopping and $B$ is a constant. This implies that
$\sigma_{\mathrm{e}}=(A / T) \exp \left(-E_{\mathrm{H}} / k T\right)$

Thus, $E_{\mathrm{H}}$ is the activation energy of both the electron mobility and the electronic conductivity. The detailed expressions and explanations of the constants $A$ and $B$ are given in the paper of Tuller and Nowick [76].

Naik and Tien [77] used a slightly different expression for the small polaron hopping
$\sigma_{\mathrm{e}}=\left(\sigma_{\mathrm{o}} / T^{3 / 2}\right) \exp \left(-E_{\mathrm{H}} / k T\right)$
and showed that this explained their data well.

### 4.2. Ionic conductivity

Whereas the ionic conductivity is always much lower than the electronic conductivity in pure reduced ceria, the situation is quite different in ceria doped with oxides of two- or three-valent metals due to the introduction of oxide ion vacancies, cf. Reactions (2.2) and (2.3). As a high vacancy concentration will shift Reaction (2.2) to the left, the electronic conductivity in air may be very low, and the doped cerias are under these conditions excellent electrolytes. The conductivity mechanism is the hopping of oxide ions to vacant sites, and the ionic conductivity, $\sigma_{\mathrm{i}}$, may be expressed as

$$
\sigma_{\mathrm{i}}=\left(\sigma_{\mathrm{o}} / T\right) \exp \left(-E_{\mathrm{H}} / k T\right)
$$

It has been found that the total conductivity consists of a contribution from the bulk of the grains (crystals or crystallites) and one from the grain boundaries, which are often very badly conducting. The two contributions are in series, i.e., a low grain boundary conductivity results in a low total con-
ductivity. Below we give some examples on how the ionic conductivity of doped ceria may behave.

### 4.2.1. Grain boundary conductivity

In the review of Kilner and Steele [4] it is described how the grain boundary resistance may occur due to, for example, an amorphous glassy phase in the grain boundaries caused by impurities. Other reasons mentioned are microporosity in the boundaries or segregation of the dopant ions. Riess et al. [82] measured the ionic conductivity of $\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\right)_{0.82}\left(\mathrm{GdO}_{1.5}\right)_{0.18}$ prepared by different methods and showed that both the ionic conductivity and activation energy were dependent upon the powder preparation route. Some grain boundary resistance is most often seen, even in high purity material, especially at low temperature.

Wang and Nowick [83] separated the total conductivity of $\mathrm{Y}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ and CaO doped $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ in bulk and grain boundary contributions. The presence of relatively high-resistivity material along the grain boundaries caused an extra arc in the impedance spectra. The effect was greatest for relatively dilute solid solutions and almost disappeared for dopant concentrations greater than approximately $15 \mathrm{~mol} \%$. ### 4.2.2. Total ionic conductivity

Most of the early, and a lot of later, works were carried out using classical four-point conductivity measurements. This gives the total conductivity of the sample, with small possibilities of resolving it into the bulk and the grain boundary conductivities. This, however, can be done by means of a.c. impedance analysis using two electrodes only. Under favourable conditions it is possible to derive the grain boundary resistances from the measured impedance spectra.

The total d.c. conductivity is the important quanti-
ty for practical applications, and, as some general features were observed from these measurements, examples of such work are described below.

### 4.2.3. Bulk ionic conductivity

The observation that the activation energy is a strong function of dopant concentration at low temperature was also found for the pure bulk conductivity [106,107]. Fig. 29 shows the conductivity of yttria doped ceria as a function of dopant con-
centration, and in the same figure the variation in the activation enthalpy is also shown. The suggested explanation is as follows. The oxygen vacancies may form associates with the dopant cations of the type $\left\{\mathrm{Y}_{\mathrm{Ce}} \mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{O}}\right\}^{\cdot}$. The decrease in activation enthalpy in the dilute range is due to a decrease in association enthalpy as a result of electrostatic interaction, and the increase in the high concentration range is due to the development of deep vacancy traps as a result of defect ordering and formation of microdomains.

Faber et al. [108] investigated the variation of the activation energy for conduction, as a function of the dopant ion type and concentration in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$. For all five dopants studied, a plot of activation energy versus dopant concentration showed a minimum (see Fig. 30). They described this minimum in terms of attractive interactions between immobile dopant ions and mobile oxygen vacancies. Their explanation is similar to Wang et al.'s. When the amount of dopant is increased, the oxide vacancies interact with several dopant ions. The energy of an oxide ion in a saddlepoint between sites with interactions with dopant ions will have a lower energy than in a saddle-point between sites with no interaction. (The activation energy of the oxide ion mobility is the extra energy which is necessary to bring the oxide ion from a regular site to the saddle-point). At higher dopant concentrations more deep traps consisting of dopant ions next to each other are formed, and the activation energy increases. The composition associated with the minimum of the activation energy is determined by the range of interaction of the dopant ion with the mobile oxygen ion. It is necessary to consider the interaction of a vacancy out to a distance of third or fourth nearest neighbours. It is further concluded that the charge and the size are not the only parameters which determine the interaction range.

Huang et al. [109] studying $\mathrm{Ce}_{0.9} \mathrm{Gd}_{0.1} \mathrm{O}_{1.95}$ found that below a temperature, $T^{*}=583 \pm 45^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ the system could be modelled by a condensation of mobile oxide vacancies into ordered clusters. The trapping energy is $\Delta H_{\mathrm{t}}\left(1-T / T^{*}\right)$ and $\Delta H_{\mathrm{t}}=0.19 \pm 0.01 \mathrm{eV}$. The activation enthalpy for oxide ion motion was found to be $0.63 \pm 0.01 \mathrm{eV}$.

### 4.2.4. Defect interaction models

Atlas [113] proposed a statistical model to account for the partial ordering of anion vacancies and reduced cations in the substoichiometric $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ com-
pound. The author showed a semiquantitative agreement between the proposed model and the work of Bevan and Kordis [32].

Tuller and Nowick [114] derived from measurements a model for pure $\mathrm{CeO}_{2-x}$. For $x<10^{-3}$ the ideal situation with non-interacting small polarons and doubly ionised oxide vacancies prevails. For $10^{-3}<x<10^{-2}$ singly ionised vacancies (i.e. a small polaron associated with the oxide vacancy, which is regarded as one defect) are dominant. For even larger $x$-values defect interaction takes place. They calculated the enthalpy of formation of singly and doubly ionised vacancies to be 4.1 eV and 4.7 eV , respectively, ( 396 and $454 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}$ ). Assuming only pairwise defect interaction, they obtained a defect interaction energy of $0.055 \mathrm{eV}\left(5.3 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\right)$.

Butler et al. [115] and Catlow [116] modelled the effect of the dopant ion radius on the dopant-vacancy interaction in $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$, and thus on the ionic conductivity. The authors calculated the binding energy of oxide vacancies with an oxygen vacancy with one, two, three and four nearest neighbour dopant ions for dopant ions with different ion size and charge. ## 5. Practical applications in electrochemical devices

### 5.1. Ceria as an SOFC electrolyte

Takahashi and Iwahara [126] investigated $\mathrm{La}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ doped $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ as an electrolyte in an SOFC. Although the authors observed a high power density with this material, they observed open circuit voltages lower than theoretical due to partial reduction of the electrolyte by the fuel. Yahiro et al. [127] fabricated a high temperature SOFC using $\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\right)_{0.8}\left(\mathrm{YO}_{1.5}\right)_{0.2}$ as the electrolyte and $\mathrm{La}_{0.6} \mathrm{Sr}_{0.4} \mathrm{CoO}_{3}$ as the cathode. They also showed good performance characteristics. Inoue et al. [128] fabricated an SOFC using an electrolyte based on $\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\right)_{0.8}\left(\mathrm{SmO}_{1.5}\right)_{0.2}$, and Pt electrodes. The authors, however, observed that the open circuit voltage was low due to reduction of the electrolyte by the $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ fuel. They also showed that if a protective layer of YSZ (put down using RF sputtering) was coated on the fuel side of the SOFC, then the reduction of the electrolyte was suppressed. This cell showed good performance. The incentive for making such a cell may be limited because cells with anode or cathode supported thin electrolytes of YSZ have been demonstrated. In spite of this, the results are interesting because they show that ceria-zirconia interfaces may be formed without causing adverse effects. This means that doped ceria, for example, may be used as a barrier layer for high-performance
$\mathrm{LaCoO}_{3}$ based cathodes, which react adversely with zirconia.

A number of more recent publications have examined the use of doped $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ as a possible SOFC electrolyte [129-132]. Many of the more recent publications have concentrated on improving the synthesis and fabrication processes of doped $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$. Park et al. [133] studied $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}-\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ powders synthesised using a glycine-nitrate process, while Van herle et al. [134,135] have studied a number of doped ceria powders (Y, Gd, and Sm) using a coprecipitation technique. A review of the fabrication methods is not within the scope of this paper and is a subject for its own review paper, which we hope somebody will undertake in the near future.

### 5.2. Ceria as SOFC anode

Back in 1964, Möbius and Rohland [136] showed that ceria could work as an anode in an SOFC fuelled with $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ and CO . Takahashi et al. [137] described $\mathrm{Ce}_{0.6} \mathrm{Y}_{0.4} \mathrm{O}_{1.8}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{0.6} \mathrm{La}_{0.4} \mathrm{O}_{1.8}$ as being excellent anode materials for SOFC. Much later, this work was continued with the purpose of using ceria based materials as an anode for direct oxidation of dry methane [138-143]. Cells with a ceria anode had a performance similar to cells with Ni-YSZ-electrodes in hydrogen. Also in methane good performances were reported when Pt or Ni metals were present. Originally, Mogensen et al. [140] believed that reduced ceria was a good electrocatalyst for direct methane oxidation. This was based on the observation that in blind tests with the Pt current collector directly on the YSZ electrolyte, a very high polarisation resistance was obtained, and thus it was concluded that Pt was almost inert for the reactions in question as also reported by Steele et al. [144]. The tricky point is, however, that platinum is a good methane cracking catalyst. Thus, it was later shown by the same group that if an inert gold current collector is used, then the ceria based anode is equally good in hydrogen but almost inert in methane, and hence a cracking catalyst must be present. In spite of this, we believe that there is real potential in ceria as an anode for conversion of hydrocarbon fuels, because ceria can tolerate carbon precipitation and is able to oxidise the carbon. In this context it should be remembered that one of the
oldest applications of ceria has been as a carbon oxidation catalyst, and still today it is used as a catalyst in self-cleaning ovens and for the oxidation of diesel soot in automobiles [8].

Ceria has also been used as an addition (or as the only ceramic) in nickel - or ruthenium - cermet anodes for hydrogen oxidation. Schmidberger [145], Eguchi et al. [146], Watanabe et al. [147], Uchida et al. [148,149] and Tsai and Barnett [150] have all reported beneficial effects probably due to the broadening of the three-phase boundary zone width as pointed out by Schouler and Kleitz [151]. Basic studies of the electrocatalytic activity of doped ceria were carried out by Horita et al. [152].

### 5.3. Mechanical-thermodynamical considerations

In Section 3 it was described how both doped and undoped ceria expand on reduction. This phenomenon will cause mechanical stresses in the ceria in cases where it is used as an electrolyte, where the anode side is reduced to a smaller or larger degree. If the ceria is used as an anode the expansion will cause stresses at the anode-electrolyte interface and may, unless special precautions are taken, cause a delamination of the anode. To what extent this is a practical problem is, of course, a matter of the balance between the mechanical strength of the ceria and the local expansion at the conditions in question. Thus, in order to assess the problem it is necessary to also have knowledge of the mechanical properties of ceria.

### 5.3.1. Mechanical properties of ceria and doped ceria materials

Mashino et al. [153] found that cubic $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ has a room temperature four-point bend strength of $<100$ MPa and a fracture toughness of approximately 1.5 MPa $\mathrm{m}^{1 / 2}$. Recently, Zhang [154], Sammes et al. [155], and Bellon et al. [156] have examined the mechanical properties of $\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\right)_{0.8}\left(\mathrm{GdO}_{1.5}\right)_{0.2}$ electrolyte materials. Using a four-point bend test method on polished bars of $\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\right)_{0.8}\left(\mathrm{GdO}_{1.5}\right)_{0.2}$, the bending strength was found to be $143 \pm 10 \mathrm{MPa}$ at room temperature and $115 \pm 12 \mathrm{MPa}$ at $800^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. The bending strength and fracture toughness of this material was carried out on bars fabricated using the standard solid state technique on precipitated powders. The fracture toughness was determined to be $2.48 \mathrm{MPa} \mathrm{m}^{1 / 2}$ or below, depending on the method used. In general, fracture of ceramics is caused by flaws originating from the fabrication, and thus, the fabrication method is of most importance. Bellon et al. [156] fabricated thin tubes and rods of $\left(\mathrm{CeO}_{2}\right)_{0.8}\left(\mathrm{GdO}_{1.5}\right)_{0.2}$ by extruding a dried slurry. A room temperature modulus of rupture (MOR) of 220 MPa was realised for this material, after firing at $1600^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for 10 h . A sintering temperature of at least $1400^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for 10 h was necessary to achieve a density of $>90 \%$, required for SOFC applications. The essential data are collected in Table 6.

### 5.4. Compatibility of doped ceria with other SOFC components

### 5.4.1. Compatibility with YSZ

Several workers have studied coating of YSZ on $\mathrm{CeO}_{2}$ electrolytes [127,158,159]. The intention was to avoid the reduction of ceria, and by this avoid the electronic conductivity. These works showed that the diffusion of ceria and zirconia into each other did not take place within periods of days at temperatures below about $1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. The reactions of doped ceria with YSZ have been studied by Tompsett et al. [160], Dokiya [161] and Sammes et al. [162]. Tompsett et al. studied the reaction of $\mathrm{Ce}_{1-x} \mathrm{Gd}_{x} \mathrm{O}_{2-y}$ with 8YSZ, in which polished pellets were held in intimate contact at elevated temperatures of up to $1300^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for 72 h . Also samples of the powdered materials mixed in a $1: 1$ ratio (weight basis) were formed into pellets and fired at elevated temperatures. These workers found that reaction was observed only after heat treatment at $1300^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for 72 h. The formation of a third cubic-like phase was observed using XRD and Raman spectroscopy and a diffusion layer of up to $20 \mu \mathrm{~m}$ thickness was observed at the interface, using energy dispersive x-ray analysis. Horita et al. [163] also reported the presence of an interfacial phase between doped ceria
and YSZ layers. This interfacial phase degraded the conductivity of the composite electrolyte system. At operating temperatures $\left(<1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)$ there will be little interfacial phase formation. However, at higher temperatures, which may be used in the fabrication of composite electrolytes, it will be significant.

Sammes et al. [162] report the formation of single cubic phases over a range of compositions of $\mathrm{Ce}_{0.8} \mathrm{Gd}_{0.2} \mathrm{O}_{2-x}-\mathrm{YSZ}$ with an immiscibility gap close to $50 \mathrm{~mol} \% 8 \mathrm{YSZ}$.

### 5.4.2. Compatibility with other SOFC materials

Sahibzada et al. [164] revealed that when a NiYSZ anode was sintered at $1300^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ onto a $\mathrm{Ce}(\mathrm{Gd}) \mathrm{O}_{2-x}$ electrolyte, the formation of $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{Zr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{7}$ and $\mathrm{Gd}_{2} \mathrm{NiO}_{4}$ were observed. These compounds were thought to contribute to the poor cell performance, due to their high resistivities.

Hatchwell et al. [165] studied the chemical compatibility of chromium based interconnects and related materials with gadolinia doped ceria. XRD and scanning electron microscopy were used to determine the reaction products of (La, Sr$) \mathrm{CrO}_{3}, \mathrm{SrCrO}_{4}$, $\mathrm{CaCrO}_{4}$ and $\mathrm{Cr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ with gadolinia doped ceria (CGO). Powder mixtures and multilayer pellets were annealed in air at temperatures of $650-1600^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for up to 400 h . No reaction was observed between $(\mathrm{La}, \mathrm{Sr}) \mathrm{CrO}_{3}$ and $\mathrm{Ce}_{0.8} \mathrm{Gd}_{0.2} \mathrm{O}_{1.9}$ after 10 h at $1600^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. However, $\mathrm{SrCrO}_{4}$ reacted with CGO via a $\mathrm{Sr}-\mathrm{Cr}-\mathrm{O}$ liquid phase forming an unknown phase at the interface and grain boundaries, consisting of Ce , $\mathrm{Sr}, \mathrm{Gd}$ and Cr . Similarly, the $\mathrm{CaCrO}_{4}$ phase reacted with CGO via a liquid Ca-Cr-O phase, transforming CGO grains into an unknown phase. $\mathrm{Cr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$ was shown to be compatible with CGO for temperatures below $1400^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ in air.

## 6. Concluding remarks

We think that ceria is an intriguing material. It holds a lot of potential for many applications, but it is also a fairly complicated material. It is our hope that this paper may contribute to the inspiration for further work, which will improve our knowledge about ceria and help us in making practical applications.
