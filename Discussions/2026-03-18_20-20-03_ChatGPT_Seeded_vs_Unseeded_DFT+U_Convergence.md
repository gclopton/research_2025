# Seeded vs Unseeded DFT+U Convergence

> [!question] Seeded vs Unseeded DFT+U Convergence
> I'm running DFT+U plane-wave calculations on Ce7O12 (Ce21O36 supercell, 57 atoms) using VASP with the following settings: ISPIN=2, LDAUTYPE=2, LDAUU=5.0 on Ce 4f, ISMEAR=0, SIGMA=0.02, EDIFF=1E-4, conservative mixing (AMIX=0.2, BMIX=0.0001).
>
> I have two scenarios for an ENCUT convergence sweep (400-800 eV):
>
> Unseeded: Each ENCUT point starts from scratch (ISTART=0, ICHARG=2) with no WAVECAR seed.
>
> Seeded: Each ENCUT point starts from a pre-converged WAVECAR generated at ENCUT=900 eV for the same structure and magnetic configuration.
>
> The seeded runs converge in ~35 SCF steps. The unseeded runs are taking significantly longer, with rms(c) still high after 30+ steps.
>
> My question: assuming both seeded and unseeded runs reach the same EDIFF convergence criterion, should the final results (total energy, forces, magnetic moments, onsite occupation matrices) be identical? Or is there a realistic possibility that the seeded vs unseeded starting point leads to convergence to different metastable electronic states in a DFT+U system like this?
>
> Ultimately, I want to know if using a WAVECAR seed will lead to results that are actually valid, or if it will create some kind of bias that leads the system into an incorrect solution.

Yes: there is a realistic possibility that seeded and unseeded Ce7O12 DFT+U runs converge to different self-consistent metastable electronic states, even if both satisfy the same `EDIFF` criterion.

The key point is that `EDIFF` only tells you that the SCF loop has reached a fixed point for the Kohn-Sham problem you are iterating. It does not tell you that the fixed point is unique, nor that it is the lowest-energy electronic solution available for that ionic structure. In plain PBE calculations on simple systems, different starts often collapse to the same solution. But in DFT+U with localized 4f states, especially in a mixed-valence ceria-derived cell like Ce21O36, multiple basins are entirely plausible. The DFT+U literature explicitly treats metastability as a real issue for localized d/f systems, and this has been discussed specifically for ceria/Ce(III) localization in [Allen et al.](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2014/cp/c4cp01083c).

For your specific setup, the chemistry makes this even more likely. Ce7O12 is not a simple closed-shell oxide; it contains reduced Ce environments, and in DFT+U the excess 4f character can localize on different Ce sites and in different orbital patterns. Allen et al. describe DFT+U metastability as arising from different orbitals and different locations of localized electrons, including Ce(III)/ceria examples. [Fabris et al.](https://link.aps.org/pdf/10.1103/PhysRevB.71.041102?utm_source=chatgpt.com) likewise reported that defective ceria exhibits a multitude of metastable low-energy states.

So the answer breaks into two cases. If the seeded and unseeded calculations converge to the same electronic branch, then the final observables should agree to within ordinary numerical noise. In that case, total energies, forces, site moments, and DFT+U onsite occupations should be essentially the same, up to tiny differences from SCF history, mixer history, and diagonalization details. For an ENCUT sweep, VASP actually recommends [`ISTART=1`](https://vasp.at/wiki/ISTART) when changing the cutoff, because it redefines the plane-wave basis consistently for the new `ENCUT`; the wiki says this is the usual setting for cutoff convergence tests.

But if the seed causes the run to stay on a different metastable branch, then all of those quantities can differ in a physically meaningful way: total energy can differ by meV to much more, local Ce moments can rearrange, and the onsite occupation matrices can land in a different localized pattern. That is not a pathology of the WAVECAR itself; it is the normal consequence of a multi-minimum DFT+U landscape, again as discussed in [Allen et al.](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2014/cp/c4cp01083c).

So using a WAVECAR seed is not invalid and is not automatically biasing you into a wrong solution. In fact, for what you are doing, it is often the right thing to do. If your goal is an ENCUT convergence test on one fixed physical electronic state, then seeding from a high-quality converged solution is usually preferable, because it helps each ENCUT point remain on the same branch instead of letting each point wander into a different 4f-localization pattern. VASP's own documentation explicitly endorses restart-based cutoff tests and also notes that restarting from a partially converged `WAVECAR` can improve electronic convergence; see [`ISTART`](https://vasp.at/wiki/ISTART).

The subtlety is that a restart is good for branch tracking, but not sufficient by itself for ground-state discovery. Those are different tasks. For basis-set convergence, you generally want branch tracking. For finding the physically relevant lowest-energy state, you should also test alternative electronic initializations.

For your Ce7O12 workflow, the practical interpretation is this: a `WAVECAR` seed from `ENCUT=900 eV` is a valid way to run the `400-800 eV` sweep, and it is probably the cleaner way to do it if the purpose is to assess basis convergence of one chosen spin/localization pattern. It does not inherently corrupt the results. But you should not assume that the seeded solution is the only possible one just because it converges quickly. In a Ce 4f DFT+U problem, the seed can absolutely determine which metastable solution you land in, as emphasized by [Allen et al.](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2014/cp/c4cp01083c).

What I would trust as a rigorous workflow is this. Use the seeded sweep as your main ENCUT calibration, so every point is evaluating the same electronic state. Then, at a few representative cutoffs such as 500, 650, and 800 eV, do additional checks from alternative starts: scratch starts, altered `MAGMOM`, maybe different Ce3+ localization guesses if you have them. If they all collapse back to the same final energy, moments, and occupation pattern, you can be confident the branch is robust. If they land in different states, then the issue is electronic metastability, not ENCUT convergence. In that case, compare ENCUT only within each branch, and separately decide which branch is physically relevant or lowest in energy.

Two more VASP-specific cautions matter here. First, if any part of your restart path uses `CHGCAR` rather than just `WAVECAR`, VASP warns that DFT+U restarts can be sensitive to `LMAXMIX`, because only on-site densities up to `LMAXMIX` are written; for f systems it recommends `LMAXMIX=6`. Without that, restarted DFT+U energies can differ noticeably from a fully self-consistent run; see [`ICHARG`](https://www.vasp.at/wiki/ICHARG) and [`LDAU`](https://vasp.at/wiki/LDAU). Second, if you are restarting with `ISTART=1` and leaving `ICHARG` unspecified, VASP defaults to `ICHARG=0`, meaning it builds the initial density from the initial wavefunctions. That is a true wavefunction-seeded continuation. VASP also notes that for repeated runs with small parameter changes, `ICHARG=1` can reduce the number of electronic steps, but then the `LMAXMIX` warning becomes especially important for f-electron DFT+U; again see [`ICHARG`](https://www.vasp.at/wiki/ICHARG).

So the clean answer is: using a `WAVECAR` seed is scientifically valid for your ENCUT sweep, and for branch-consistent convergence testing it is often preferable. But in Ce7O12 DFT+U, seeded and unseeded runs are not guaranteed to end at the same final solution. If they do, the final observables should match closely. If they do not, that is evidence of genuine DFT+U metastability, not evidence that the seed is wrong.

> [!question] Lowest Branch and MLIP Accuracy
> No it is not necessarily bad if they end up at a different final solution?
>
> We are doing these DFT calibrations in preparation for training MLIPs. After plane-wave and kpoints convergence tests, I will be running AIMD to generate a dataset containing energies and forces.
>
> We will be using that data to train our MLIPs. These MLIPs will be used for LAMMPS simulations to simulate track formation and track annealing processes in LAMMPS.
>
> If, I am correct, I believe it is the case that CeO2 is more resistant to damage from swift heavy ions than UO2 (according to work by Lang). We have a hypothesis that it is the redox chemistry of ceria that leads to this difference and causes annealing of swift heavy ion tracks.
>
> But then theres this question: If we don't land in the branch that is the lowest state (i.e. because we used this seed method) would that really matter in the grand scheme of things in terms of accuracy of our MLIPs?

No, not automatically.

For an MLIP, the deepest requirement is not "every label must come from the absolute lowest electronic state at all costs." The deeper requirement is that your reference data define a single, internally consistent surface that maps atomic positions to one energy and one force field. Standard MLIPs are usually trained to the ground-state Born-Oppenheimer surface, with forces as derivatives of that surface; when additional electronic degrees of freedom matter, such as magnetic states or excited states, the problem becomes harder precisely because there may no longer be just one position-only surface. That broader issue is discussed in [this review](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.09814).

That is why, in your case, the main danger is not "I used a `WAVECAR`, therefore my data are biased." The main danger is that Ce 4f DFT+U may admit multiple low-energy self-consistent solutions for the same or nearby geometries, with different site/orbital localizations of the extra 4f electrons. That is a known feature of ceria-like DFT+U calculations, not a pathology of your workflow. [Fabris et al.](https://link.aps.org/doi/10.1103/PhysRevB.71.041102?utm_source=chatgpt.com) report that defective ceria has a multitude of metastable low-energy states, and [Allen et al.](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2014/cp/c4cp01083c) explicitly discuss metastability from different orbital occupations and different localization sites in Ce systems.

So if your seeded workflow keeps the whole AIMD trajectory on one coherent branch, then from the MLIP point of view that can actually be a good thing. In that case, you are teaching the model one smooth adiabatic surface rather than letting each snapshot randomly collapse into a different Ce3+ localization pattern depending on SCF history. For ENCUT convergence specifically, VASP even recommends [`ISTART=1`](https://vasp.at/wiki/ISTART?utm_source=chatgpt.com) when the cutoff changes and says that this is the usual setting for cutoff convergence tests.

Where it becomes bad is when the dataset is branch-mixed. Then two very similar local structures can carry labels from different electronic solutions, so the training set is no longer a clean single-valued PES. That kind of inconsistency is qualitatively different from ordinary numerical noise. Recent MLIP work does show that you can often tolerate lower numerical convergence in the DFT labels and still get strong MLIPs, especially because forces often converge faster than total energies, but that is about controlled label noise on one surface, not about sampling two different DFT+U branches that represent different underlying surfaces; see [this recent discussion](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlehtml/2026/dd/d5dd00294j).

For your ceria irradiation hypothesis, this matters even more because the redox physics is not incidental; it is part of the mechanism you care about. The irradiation literature you mentioned is broadly consistent with the idea that redox behavior is coupled to damage response: CeO2 tends to reduce under swift heavy ion irradiation, while UO2 tends to oxidize under similar conditions, and this redox response is linked to radiation tolerance and structural modification, as discussed in [Tracy et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms7133). If the wrong branch merely means you stayed on one physically reasonable Ce3+ localization pattern instead of another nearly degenerate one, the MLIP may still be perfectly usable for that branch. But if the seed systematically suppresses the very valence/localization changes that are central to your annealing mechanism, then yes, that would matter in the grand scheme.

So the right standard is not "lowest branch or bust." It is closer to this: the branch must be physically relevant and dataset-wide consistent. For equilibrium ground-state properties, the lowest branch is usually the safest target. For a nonequilibrium materials problem, a metastable branch can still be legitimate if it is the branch your adiabatic dynamics actually follows over the region of configuration space you sample. What you do not want is a training set where the branch assignment changes arbitrarily from snapshot to snapshot because of initialization accidents rather than physics. That is exactly the motivation behind the occupation-matrix-control discussion in [Allen et al.](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2014/cp/c4cp01083c).

One more honest caveat: a standard geometry-only MLIP, even a very good one, is still learning an effective adiabatic surface. The initial electronically excited part of swift-heavy-ion damage is not something a conventional ground-state MLIP captures directly; that is one reason people combine atomistic potentials with separate electronic-energy-deposition models, as discussed in [this review](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.09814).

My bottom-line answer is that using the seed is not inherently bad, and landing on a metastable branch is not automatically fatal for MLIP accuracy. It becomes a real problem only when that branch is physically wrong for the phenomenon you want, or when your dataset mixes multiple branches in an uncontrolled way.

> [!question] SRIM and Electronic-Energy Deposition Models
> Wait, what do you mean by this?
>
> One more honest caveat: a standard geometry-only MLIP, even a very good one, is still learning an effective adiabatic surface. The initial electronically excited part of swift-heavy-ion damage is not something a conventional ground-state MLIP captures directly; that is one reason people combine atomistic potentials with separate electronic-energy-deposition models. So branch consistency in your DFT+U data is essential, but it is only one layer of the larger modeling approximation.
>
> Is there something that I am failing to look at here? What do you mean by separate electronic-energy-deposition models? We are using SRIM, is that what you mean?

Yes: SRIM is part of what I meant, but not the whole thing.

What I was trying to say is that a conventional MLIP in LAMMPS gives you an interatomic force law for atoms moving on a chosen DFT-derived surface. By itself, it does not know that a swift heavy ion first dumps most of its energy into the electronic subsystem, not directly into atomic motion. In the SHI regime, the projectile mainly loses energy by inelastic interactions with target electrons, producing a dense trail of electronic excitations; the direct projectile-atom collision channel is often much smaller and can even be neglected in first approximation. SRIM is commonly used to estimate those electronic and nuclear stopping powers, as described in [this review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168583X14000974).

So when I said separate electronic-energy-deposition models, I meant the extra physics layer that answers questions like: how is the electronic energy deposited around the ion path; over what radial profile; how does it evolve in time; and how quickly is it transferred from excited electrons to the lattice? That is exactly the role of the inelastic thermal spike model and related two-temperature or two-temperature molecular dynamics (2TM, TTM, 2T-MD) frameworks. Toulemonde's review describes track formation in terms of the initial electronic energy deposition plus its later diffusion through the electronic and lattice subsystems, with the track outcome controlled by the energy density delivered to atoms and the electron-phonon coupling strength. Related discussion of 2T-MD appears in [this article](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168583X11011645).

So the clean separation is this. SRIM tells you how much energy the ion loses per unit length, for example `S_e` along the trajectory, and sometimes its depth dependence. But SRIM does not by itself tell you the full atomistic nonequilibrium story of how that electronic energy becomes a transient hot cylinder, how it spreads radially, how the electrons and lattice exchange energy, or whether the excited electronic state temporarily changes bonding. Those extra assumptions are supplied by the thermal-spike or two-temperature part of the workflow. That is why many SHI simulations use a chain like SRIM -> `S_e` input -> thermal-spike or TTM calculation -> MD or TTM-MD atomic response, as discussed in [this literature](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168583X11011645?utm_source=chatgpt.com).

So are you missing something? Maybe not; it depends on what your actual workflow is.

If your workflow is essentially "use SRIM to get the stopping power, then use that to drive a cylindrical electronic-heating or TTM-style source term, then run LAMMPS with your MLIP to evolve the lattice," then you are already doing what I meant. In that case, the MLIP is the lattice-response model, while SRIM plus thermal-spike or TTM is the electronic-deposition model. That is a standard multiscale picture for SHI track simulations, again as discussed in [this work](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0168583X11011645).

But if the workflow is only "get `S_e` from SRIM, then somehow launch ordinary MD on a ground-state MLIP without a modeled electronic subsystem or explicit electron-to-lattice energy-transfer scheme," then there is a conceptual gap. In that case, you know how much electronic energy was lost, but you have not yet specified how that lost electronic energy enters the atomic dynamics. That missing bridge is what the thermal-spike or two-temperature model supplies.

There is one more nuance. Even with TTM-MD, you are still usually treating the atomic system with an interatomic potential defined on an effective adiabatic surface. [Darkins et al.](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927025618300892) note that for very highly excited electrons, simple electron-phonon coupling may not be enough, because electronic excitation can also modify the interatomic interactions themselves. In other words, a standard ground-state MLIP plus a heating/coupling model may still miss some genuinely nonadiabatic or excitation-altered bonding physics.

So the shortest accurate answer is: yes, SRIM is part of what I meant, but the broader point is that SRIM gives stopping, while the thermal-spike, TTM, or TTM-MD model gives the electron-to-lattice transfer and transient energy-density evolution that actually drives track formation and annealing in MD. If your Ce-O workflow is SRIM feeding a cylindrical TTM or i-TS model that then couples into LAMMPS, then you are not overlooking the distinction; you are already implementing it.

> [!question] Replacing the Classical Potential with an MLIP
> Oh, you are telling me something I already know. We already have that set up for the classical potential that I tested.
>
> So once we have the MLIP trained, the idea is to plug that into that system, is that how it works? The idea is that we would then have a model that could take into account the redox chemistry?

Yes: that is the basic idea.

In the workflow you already have, the SRIM plus thermal-spike, TTM, or i-TS machinery stays in place, and the thing you replace is the interatomic potential inside LAMMPS. In other words, the electronic energy deposition model is still the part that says "the ion deposits energy into the electronic subsystem like this, with this spatial and temporal profile," while the MLIP becomes the atomistic model that tells LAMMPS how the lattice responds once that energy is coupled into atomic motion. That is exactly the kind of division of labor used in two-temperature MD approaches; see [this discussion](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/am/pii/S0927025618300892?utm_source=chatgpt.com).

The part that needs careful wording is "take into account the redox chemistry." A standard MLIP trained on DFT+U energies and forces does not carry explicit electrons, and it does not propagate occupation matrices or oxidation states as independent dynamical variables. What it does learn is an effective potential-energy surface in which geometries associated with reduced and oxidized ceria can have different energies and forces. So if your training data include the relevant Ce(IV)/Ce(III), oxygen-vacancy, distorted, and hot-lattice environments, then the MLIP can absolutely encode the structural consequences of that redox physics well enough to change damage recovery, defect migration, and annealing behavior in MD. That broader issue is discussed in [this review](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.09814).

That means the answer is yes, in principle: the MLIP can let your LAMMPS/TTM setup respond in a way that reflects ceria's redox-enabled energetics, but only implicitly through the DFT+U training labels. It is not doing redox chemistry the way a quantum calculation does it on the fly. It is reproducing a DFT+U-informed force law in regions of configuration space where redox-associated local environments have already been learned.

This is also why your training set design matters so much. If the dataset spans only one narrow branch or only near-equilibrium CeO2-like environments, then the MLIP may be excellent there but useless for vacancy-rich, reduced, highly distorted track-core states. Conversely, if the dataset spans the relevant reduced, oxidized, and thermally disordered motifs that appear during track formation and annealing, then the MLIP has a real chance to capture the effective redox-assisted recovery pathway you care about. Reviews on MLIP data generation make exactly this point in broader terms: the training set has to cover the physically relevant states, and models that do not distinguish electronic states can run into trouble when multiple electronic states are mixed into one dataset without care; see [this ACS review](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acs.chemrev.4c00572?utm_source=chatgpt.com).

There is one more caveat, and it is the main one. If you have cases where the same or nearly the same atomic geometry can correspond to different Ce 4f localization patterns or different electronic branches, then a plain geometry-only MLIP may not be able to represent both cleanly as one single-valued map from coordinates to energy and forces. That is where people start thinking about more specialized models, including charge-aware MLIPs, oxidation-state-aware treatments, or other extensions that introduce extra electronic information beyond just positions and species. Those kinds of methods are being developed precisely because ordinary MLIPs can struggle when long-range charge transfer or multiple oxidation patterns matter; see [this work](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-020-20427-2?utm_source=chatgpt.com).

So the practical answer for your project is this: yes, once trained, the MLIP would be plugged into the same SRIM -> TTM/i-TS -> LAMMPS framework in place of the classical potential. And yes, that can be the route by which your atomistic model starts reflecting ceria's redox-enabled energetics much better than a fixed classical potential, provided the DFT+U training data actually cover the reduced and damaged states that matter for SHI tracks. What it will not do, unless you build something more specialized, is carry explicit electronic occupations as live dynamical variables during the simulation.

The cleanest mental model is that your TTM/i-TS machinery handles where and when the energy enters the lattice, and the MLIP handles how a redox-capable ceria lattice responds once that energy is there. > [!question] Redox Chemistry and Missing Track Theory
> So if by adding this new MLIP we find that that redox chemistry does in fact matter in the formation of the track, then does that hint at a missing term in equations that are used to describe track formation? Like some kind of term into account the redox chemistry? Maybe this will give a hint that the lattice Hamiltonian needs a term that is directly concerned with redox chemistry?
>
> Basically, I am wondering if our study will point to the fact that there is something missing in the underlying theory, leading us to work out a new term that describes this physics that we are investigating here (which would probably be a separate theoretical paper)

Yes: that is a plausible outcome, but I would phrase it a little differently.

If the MLIP changes your predicted track formation or annealing relative to the old classical potential, the first conclusion is usually not "the fundamental track equations are missing a term." The first conclusion is more modest: the old atomistic model did not represent the relevant Ce-O energetics well enough. Two-temperature or thermal-spike frameworks already have the broad nonequilibrium structure in place, including electronic energy deposition, electron-lattice coupling, and lattice evolution, but the review literature is explicit that one of the hard parts is how electronic excitation can effectively modify the interatomic interactions used inside the atomistic model; see [this discussion](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927025618300892).

That said, if the MLIP result is specifically that Ce redox flexibility changes the track outcome under otherwise identical deposited energy, then your study could absolutely point to something missing at the level of model closure. The key experimental fact already exists: in highly ionizing irradiation, CeO2 shows irradiation-induced reduction and that redox behavior is reported to be coupled to structural modification and radiation tolerance. [Tracy et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms7133) go so far as to say that tolerance depends on the efficiency of this redox reaction.

In that situation, the missing ingredient is probably not a single extra additive scalar in a bare lattice Hamiltonian $H(\mathbf R)$. It is more likely that the effective theory needs an additional state variable or internal field describing the material's redox state, such as a local Ce3+/Ce4+ fraction, oxygen nonstoichiometry, vacancy concentration, or some related order parameter. In atomistic language, that means you may need to go from a single-valued coordinate-only surface $U(\mathbf R)$ to something more like $U(\mathbf R,\eta)$, where $\eta$ encodes redox or electronic-state information. In continuum language, it means the thermal-spike or TTM equations may need to be coupled to an evolution law for $\eta(\mathbf r,t)$, with material coefficients and reaction energetics depending on $\eta$. That is an inference, but it is exactly the direction suggested by recent work on electron-temperature-dependent potentials and redox- or charge-aware MLIPs, such as [this paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41524-023-01168-4).

So the theoretical advance, if it emerges, would likely look more like a coupled chemo-thermo-electronic track model than one new redox term in the lattice Hamiltonian. For example, the redox variable could enter by changing defect formation energies, oxygen mobility, local elastic response, electron-phonon coupling, heat capacity, or by contributing a reaction enthalpy or source term to the energy balances. The conservation laws themselves may remain the same, while the constitutive laws and free-energy functional become richer. That distinction matters: many missing-physics results in materials theory do not require a new fundamental equation, but rather a better state description and better coupling terms. The current SHI/2T-MD literature already points in that direction by noting that electronic excitation can alter the PES itself, not just heat the lattice; again see [this article](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927025618300892).

There is also a more conservative possibility. Suppose your MLIP predicts different tracks simply because it gives more accurate bond breaking, vacancy energetics, and defect migration barriers on the same adiabatic surface than the classical potential did. Then the result would still be important, but it would not yet force a new continuum term. It would mean the old potential was an inadequate closure for the existing framework. To justify a separate theory paper, you would want to show something stronger: that there is a systematic dependence of track formation on a redox descriptor that cannot be absorbed into "just use a better potential." The motivation for that kind of richer description is consistent with [Tracy et al.](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms7133).

A strong theoretical paper would therefore look something like this in substance. First, you would show that under the same SRIM/TTM energy-deposition conditions, a fixed-valence classical potential and a redox-capable MLIP predict qualitatively different recovery or track radii. Second, you would identify a measurable internal variable, say local Ce3+ fraction, oxygen vacancy content, or a redox order parameter extracted from DFT+U labels, that correlates with the difference. Third, you would coarse-grain that observation into a reduced model where the thermal-spike dynamics are coupled to that variable. At that point, you really would have evidence for a missing state equation or coupling law in the standard track theory. Recent work on oxidation-state-aware and charge-transfer-aware MLIPs, such as [this paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41524-025-01709-z), shows that people are already moving toward exactly this kind of enriched state description when ordinary MLIPs become too restrictive.

So my answer is yes: your study could absolutely hint at missing underlying theory, but the most likely missing thing is not a mysterious extra term added by hand to $H_{\text{lattice}}$. It is more likely an omitted redox degree of freedom and its couplings to the existing thermal-spike or lattice model. If your results line up that way, that really could support a separate theory paper.

A compact way to say it is this: if the MLIP matters only because it is more accurate, you have a better potential; if it matters because track evolution depends on a hidden redox variable that existing models do not evolve, then you may have found a missing piece of the theory.
